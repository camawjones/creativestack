# Creative Strategy Output Formats

Two outputs every time: a **research pack** (the long form, evidence-cited
material) and a **Strategy on a Page** (the single-page distillation). The
Strategy on a Page shape varies by framework — see `frameworks.md` for the
per-framework shape.

The strategy *type* drives the research pack template. The five types:

- **Brand strategy** — who the brand is, what it stands for, who it's for, why
  it exists
- **Comms strategy** — how the brand talks; message hierarchy; channel logic
- **Creative platform** — the unifying creative thought that holds work together
  over years
- **Campaign strategy** — strategy for a single campaign with a specific objective
- **Category strategy** — the bigger play within the category (take share, define
  category, create new category)

---

## Research Pack — universal sections (always included)

```markdown
# Strategy Research: {Project / Client Name}
**Strategy type:** {Brand / Comms / Creative platform / Campaign / Category}
**Framework:** {chosen framework}
**Researched:** {date}
**Upstream data used:** {list skills that fed in, or "None — standalone research"}

## Headline read
{One paragraph. The most important thing the strategist needs to know before
reading anything else. Not a summary — a *take*. If the rest of the document
disappeared, this paragraph would still earn its place.}

## Cultural context
{What's happening in the wider culture that's relevant to this brief. If a trend
report was run earlier, reference those findings directly and build on them
rather than restating.}

- **{Trend / shift / movement}** — {description with source link}
- **{Trend / shift}** — {description with source link}

**Trend velocity:** {Which of these are accelerating vs established? Accelerating
trends reward early movers and punish late ones.}

## Competitor landscape
{How competitors are positioning. If `/competitor-audit` ran
earlier, summarise its findings and build — don't re-list.}

| Competitor | Positioning | Visual / verbal codes | Tone | Gap they're leaving |
|-----------|------------|----------------------|------|--------------------|
| {brand} | {positioning} | {codes} | {tone} | {gap} |

**The category default look / sound:** {What the brand will look and sound like
if it doesn't make a deliberate choice. Knowing the default is the precondition
for breaking it.}

## Audience reality
{Not personas. What the audience *actually* says, does, fears, and contradicts.
Pulled from review sites, subreddits, sales calls, customer service transcripts,
and qualitative research where available.}

- **What they say:** {direct quotes if possible}
- **What they do:** {observable behaviour, often different from what they say}
- **What they fear:** {the unspoken anxiety in the category}
- **What contradicts itself:** {the gap between stated preferences and behaviour}

## Evidence base
{Hard data points that back the strategic claims. Every claim in the Strategy
on a Page should be traceable to one of these. Cite source quality.}

| Claim | Evidence | Source | Confidence |
|-------|---------|--------|------------|
| {claim} | {data point} | {source + URL + date} | {High / Med / Low} |

## Confidence notes
{Where research was thin, where conclusions are speculative, where the data was
strong. Don't hide weakness — surface it so the strategist can pressure-test
later.}

{Then add the strategy-type-specific section below.}
```

## Brand strategy — type-specific sections

```markdown
## Brand reality check
{A grounded read on the brand as it exists today, not as the brand book wants it
to be. What does the audience actually associate with this brand? Where is the
gap between the brand's self-image and the audience's perception?}

## The tension or opportunity
{The single sharpest thing the strategy will be built on. If the framework is
Tension-based, this is *the* tension. If Cultural, *the* cultural shift. If
Challenger, *the* leader blind spot. If Purpose, *the* belief. One paragraph,
no hedging.}

## Strategic territories *(2-3 options, not one)*
{The brand can credibly occupy more than one place. Surface 2-3 distinct
territories the strategist can choose between. For each: name, one-line
description, one-line evidence, one-line risk.}

### Territory A: {Name}
- **What it is:** {one line}
- **Why it could work:** {one line, citing evidence}
- **Why it could fail:** {one line — the honest risk}

### Territory B: {Name}
{Same shape.}

### Territory C: {Name}
{Same shape — only if there's a third territory genuinely worth surfacing.}

## What the brand should *not* try to be
{Equally important. What territories should the brand explicitly not occupy?
Why? Often more useful than the "should" list.}
```

## Comms strategy — type-specific sections

```markdown
## Message hierarchy
{The thing the brand needs to say, said in order of importance. Most brands try
to say five things at once and end up saying nothing.}

1. **Primary message** — {the one thing}
2. **Supporting messages** — {2-3 max}
3. **Proof points** — {the evidence that backs the messages}

## Audience-by-audience nuance
{If the brief has multiple audiences, the message hierarchy shifts per audience.
Don't pretend one message lands the same with everyone.}

| Audience | What changes | What stays |
|----------|--------------|------------|
| {audience} | {nuance} | {core thread} |

## Channel logic
{Which channels carry which message, and why. Channel logic should be derived
from where the audience actually pays attention and what each channel is good
at — not from a media plan template.}

| Channel | Job | Message |
|---------|-----|---------|
| {channel} | {what it's doing in the comms ecosystem} | {message it carries} |

## Tone modulation
{How the brand voice flexes across channels and moments without losing coherence.
Same brand, different rooms.}
```

## Creative platform — type-specific sections

```markdown
## The single thought
{One sentence. Not a tag-line. The thought that the platform is built on. The
test: this thought should be true for the next 5 years and generate hundreds of
executions.}

## Why this thought, and not the obvious one
{2-3 sentences. The obvious thought is the one every other strategist would have
written from the same brief. Name it, then say why this one is sharper.}

## What this platform unlocks creatively
{What kind of work does this platform make possible? What kind of work does it
*rule out*? Both matter.}

## The generative test *(3 example expressions)*
{A platform should produce executions across formats. Sketch three:}

1. **In 30 seconds (film):** {2 lines}
2. **In a single image (poster / OOH):** {2 lines}
3. **In a button or single sentence:** {1 line}

{If you can't do all three, the "platform" is actually a tag-line.}

## The role for craft
{Where does craft do the heaviest lifting in this platform? Type, motion,
photography, voice, art direction? This guides which craft skills get prioritised
in production.}
```

## Campaign strategy — type-specific sections

```markdown
## Campaign objective
{One sentence. Specific, measurable, time-bound. "Awareness" is not an objective —
"shift unprompted brand recall in {audience} from X% to Y% over 6 months" is.}

## Behaviour we're trying to change *(if applicable)*
{What does the audience do today? What do we need them to do differently?
One verb, observable.}

## The single insight
{The one thing the campaign turns on. Not a fact — an insight. The difference:
a fact is true, an insight is *useful*.}

## The campaign idea space
{Not the idea — the space the idea will live in. A bounded territory the
creative team can explore. Specific enough to be useful, open enough to leave
room for craft.}

## Success looks like
{What happens if this campaign works? Not just metrics — what changes for the
brand, the audience, the category?}

## What would prove this wrong
{If this strategy is wrong, what evidence would show it? Strategists who can't
answer this aren't strategists, they're advocates.}
```

## Category strategy — type-specific sections

```markdown
## The category shape
{What the category looks like today. Players, conventions, structural truths.}

## Where value is moving
{What's growing? What's shrinking? What's about to be disrupted? Cite evidence.}

## The play
{The bigger move the brand can make. Three options:}

1. **Take share** — beat the leader at the category's own game (rarely the right
   answer)
2. **Define the category** — own the language, the codes, the meaning of the
   category (the most defensible play)
3. **Create a new category** — re-frame the brand into a category of its own
   where the comparison set changes (the highest-risk, highest-reward play)

For the recommended play: 3-4 sentences on what it looks like in practice and
what it requires from the business.

## What the brand has to *stop* doing
{Category strategy almost always means cutting things. What does the brand
need to walk away from to make the new play credible?}
```

## Strategy on a Page (universal — every output ends with this)

The single-page distillation. Designed to be printed, projected, or dropped into
a deck. The shape varies by framework — see `frameworks.md` for the
framework-specific shape.

Universal rules:
- One page. Seriously. If it spills, cut.
- Every line on the page is provable from the research pack.
- No jargon. No "synergies", no "weaponising", no "north star" without a specific
  meaning. If a word could be cut without changing meaning, cut it.
- The "question for creatives" at the end is *not* the answer — it's a door the
  creative team walks through. If the strategy answers the question for them, it
  isn't a strategy, it's a script.

```markdown
# {Project / Client Name} — Strategy on a Page

**Strategy type:** {type}  |  **Framework:** {framework}

**The challenge:**
{One sentence — what the brief is really asking, beneath what it says.}

**The context:**
{One sentence — the cultural / competitive / behavioural reality.}

**The {tension / cultural shift / leader blind spot / job / belief — depends on framework}:**
{One sentence — the thing the strategy turns on. Specific. Surprising.
Uncomfortable to write.}

**The territory:**
{One sentence — the strategic space the brand should play in.}

**The question for creatives:**
{One provocative question that opens the door without walking through it.}

**Supporting evidence:**
- {Strongest evidence point 1, with source}
- {Strongest evidence point 2, with source}
- {Strongest evidence point 3, with source}

**What to avoid:**
{1-2 lines — the obvious, expected, or already-owned territories. The work the
strategy is rejecting.}

**What would prove this wrong:**
{1-2 lines — the test that would falsify this strategy. If you can't answer
this, you don't have a strategy yet, you have an opinion.}
```

## Anti-slop checklist *(run before showing the user)*

Before delivering, walk through this list and fix any failures. Strategy slop is
the most common slop in any agency output — it's almost always written in a
"smart" voice that says nothing.

- [ ] **No undefended jargon.** "North star", "weaponise", "permission space",
  "DNA", "sweet spot", "winning aesthetic" — if any of these appear, they need a
  specific local meaning, not vibes.
- [ ] **No fake tensions.** Every claimed tension should be uncomfortable to
  name and specific to *this* category. If it could appear in any other strategy
  doc, cut it.
- [ ] **No "Brand X but for Y" laziness.** "Glossier for men", "Stripe for
  healthcare" — these are positioning shortcuts that read as strategy, but they
  aren't.
- [ ] **No advice the client could have written themselves.** "Be authentic",
  "stand for something", "engage your community" — table stakes, not strategy.
- [ ] **No conclusion-shaped provocations.** Provocations end with "?" and open
  questions. If the provocation is actually telling the creative team the answer,
  rewrite it.
- [ ] **Every strategic claim has at least one piece of cited evidence.** No
  free-floating opinions dressed up as insight.
- [ ] **The "what would prove this wrong" line exists and is specific.** This is
  the single best signal that the strategy is real and not theatre.
- [ ] **Plain English throughout.** If a sentence needs a strategist to explain
  it to a creative, rewrite it. The Strategy on a Page should be understandable
  to a smart 14-year-old.
- [ ] **The strategy could not be lifted wholesale to the brand's nearest
  competitor.** If it could, it's category language, not brand strategy.
