# Strategy Frameworks

The frameworks the strategist can pick from. Each one is a *lens*, not a recipe —
the work is in the synthesis. Pick the one that matches the brief, not the one
that's fashionable.

For each framework: when to use, when not to, the questions it asks, the lineage,
the failure mode, and the Strategy on a Page shape it produces.

If the user picks "Open", read the brief and mode, then pick the 1-2 most
appropriate frameworks and say *why*. Don't run all of them — that's strategy
slop in a hat.

---

## 1. Tension-based

**Lineage:** Stephen King (JWT), BBH "When the world zigs, zag", Russell Davies,
the British planning tradition.

**When to use:**
- The category has an unspoken contradiction the brand can name
- The audience says one thing and does another (most categories)
- The brief is asking "why does this brand exist?" implicitly
- You need a single sharp insight to anchor the work

**When *not* to use:**
- There isn't actually a tension — you're inventing one to look smart
- The category is too new for tensions to have settled
- The brand has no permission to occupy the tension's resolution

**Key questions:**
- What does the category say? What does the audience feel?
- What's the gap between what people *say* and what they *do*?
- What does the brand know to be true that everyone else is pretending isn't?
- What would the audience admit at 2am that they wouldn't say at 2pm?

**Failure mode:** Fake tensions. "People want quality but also affordability" is
not a tension — it's a description of every consumer in history. A real tension is
specific, surprising, and *uncomfortable* to name.

**Strategy on a Page shape:**
- The category truth (what the category says)
- The human truth (what the audience actually feels)
- The tension (the gap between them)
- The opportunity (what the brand can credibly do about it)
- The provocation (the question for creatives)

## 2. Cultural

**Lineage:** Douglas Holt (*How Brands Become Icons*), cultural strategy tradition,
the recent wave of brands like Patagonia, Liquid Death, Ben & Jerry's.

**When to use:**
- The brief is bigger than a product — it's about meaning
- The category has lost cultural relevance and needs reconnecting
- A specific cultural shift is happening that the brand can ride or lead
- The brand has an authentic right to occupy a cultural code (not all brands do)

**When *not* to use:**
- The brand has no credibility in the cultural space (cultural appropriation risk)
- The cultural moment has already peaked and the brand is late
- The strategy is being used to dress up a routine product launch as cultural

**Key questions:**
- What cultural code is shifting that this category sits inside?
- Who's already speaking to that shift, and where's the gap?
- What does the brand have *authentic* permission to say here?
- What would a cultural critic write about this category in 5 years?

**Failure mode:** "Riding" a cultural movement the brand has no credibility in.
Audiences see through it instantly. Patagonia can speak to environmentalism;
ExxonMobil cannot.

**Strategy on a Page shape:**
- The cultural shift (what's changing)
- The category's role in it (where the category sits today)
- The brand's permission (why this brand can credibly speak)
- The cultural code (the territory to claim)
- The provocation

## 3. Challenger brand

**Lineage:** Adam Morgan, *Eating the Big Fish*, eatbigfish consultancy.

**When to use:**
- The brand is #2 (or lower) in a category dominated by a clear leader
- The leader has obvious blind spots
- The brand has no budget to match the leader's reach and needs to punch above
- The brand needs to be recognised, not loved

**When *not* to use:**
- The brand *is* the leader (you can't challenge yourself credibly)
- There's no clear leader to push against
- The brand can't credibly back up the challenge with action
- The "challenge" is just whining about the leader

**Key questions:**
- Who is the leader and what are they too big to do?
- What does the leader stand for that the brand can credibly oppose?
- What's the leader's biggest unsaid contradiction?
- What will the brand do that the leader *can't* do back?

**Failure mode:** Generic challenger language ("disrupting the category", "the
underdog story") that doesn't actually identify or challenge anything specific.

**Strategy on a Page shape:**
- The leader and their stance (specific, not generic)
- Their blind spot (what they can't credibly do)
- The brand's challenge (the specific stand)
- The proof (what the brand does, not just says)
- The provocation

## 4. Jobs to be Done

**Lineage:** Clay Christensen, Tony Ulwick, *Competing Against Luck*.

**When to use:**
- Product or service brands where customers "hire" the product for a specific job
- The brief is asking "why does anyone buy this?" honestly
- You need to reframe the category from features to outcomes
- The brand is competing across categories (e.g., a snack vs a coffee vs a meeting)

**When *not* to use:**
- Pure brand or aesthetic categories (luxury, fashion, lifestyle) where the "job"
  is symbolic rather than functional and JTBD reduces it to utility
- The product genuinely has no functional job — only an emotional one

**Key questions:**
- What job is the customer hiring this product for?
- What were they doing before? Why did they fire that solution?
- When does the job arise? In what circumstance? With what anxiety?
- What does success look like in that moment for the customer?

**Failure mode:** Treating "job" as a feature list. The job is the *progress* the
customer is trying to make in their life — the milkshake job is "make my morning
commute less boring", not "deliver calories".

**Strategy on a Page shape:**
- The job (in the customer's words, not the brand's)
- The circumstance (when the job arises)
- The anxiety (what makes it hard)
- The brand's role in the job
- The provocation

## 5. Category Entry Points (CEPs)

**Lineage:** Byron Sharp, Jenni Romaniuk, Ehrenberg-Bass Institute, *How Brands
Grow* and *Building Distinctive Brand Assets*.

**When to use:**
- Mass-market brands that need broad mental availability across many moments
- Brands that have collapsed into a single use case and need to expand
- Categories where buying happens fast, with low engagement (FMCG, beverages,
  retail)
- Strategy needs to grow penetration, not loyalty

**When *not* to use:**
- Niche or considered-purchase categories where mental availability matters less
  than perceived fit
- Cultural / lifestyle brands where "mental availability across moments" misses
  the point of the brand
- Strategy needs differentiation, not memorability (CEPs is explicitly anti-
  differentiation in a way that polarises planners)

**Key questions:**
- In what moments does the category get bought? (Who, when, where, why, with what
  feeling, with whom?)
- Which CEPs does the brand currently own? Which are unclaimed?
- What distinctive brand assets help the brand get retrieved at those moments?
- How does the brand grow into more CEPs without losing the ones it owns?

**Failure mode:** Reading Sharp dogmatically and concluding "all strategy is
mental availability + distinctive assets, nothing else matters". CEPs is a powerful
lens but it's one lens.

**Strategy on a Page shape:**
- The category buying moments (the W-questions)
- CEPs the brand owns today
- CEPs to grow into (and the order)
- Distinctive assets to lean on / build
- The provocation

## 6. Behavioural science

**Lineage:** Daniel Kahneman, Richard Thaler, Rory Sutherland, *Alchemy*, Ogilvy
Behavioural Sciences Practice.

**When to use:**
- The brief is about changing a specific behaviour (sign up, switch, return,
  share, complete, persist)
- The audience has the *intention* but not the *follow-through*
- Friction is the real problem, not awareness
- The brand can intervene at a decision moment

**When *not* to use:**
- The brief is about meaning, brand-building, or long-term equity
- The behaviour is too far downstream of what the brand can actually influence
- "Behavioural science" is being used as decoration rather than a real tool

**Key questions:**
- What specific behaviour are we trying to change? (One verb, observable.)
- What does the current decision moment look like? Where is the friction?
- Which biases or heuristics are working *against* us today?
- Which biases or heuristics could we use *for* us?
- What's the smallest possible nudge that would move the behaviour?

**Failure mode:** Behavioural science as glitter. Naming a few biases ("loss
aversion! social proof!") and calling it a strategy. The work is in the specific
intervention, not the vocabulary.

**Strategy on a Page shape:**
- The behaviour to change (one verb)
- The current journey (where the friction is)
- The biases at play
- The intervention (the specific nudge)
- The provocation

## 7. Purpose-driven

**Lineage:** Simon Sinek (*Start With Why*), the post-2010 purpose wave, B Corp
movement.

**When to use:**
- The brand has an authentic purpose that's already being lived internally
- Employee engagement and recruitment matter as much as customer engagement
- The category is commoditised at the product level and meaning is the
  differentiator
- The leadership is willing to make decisions that *cost* the business in service
  of the purpose

**When *not* to use:**
- The "purpose" is being invented by marketing and isn't lived inside the
  business
- Brand can't make sacrifices in service of the purpose (purposewashing risk)
- The category is too pragmatic for purpose to feel genuine (e.g., insurance,
  utilities) — purpose can still work but the bar is much higher

**Key questions:**
- What does this brand believe to be true about the world that competitors don't?
- What would the brand willingly *sacrifice* in service of that belief?
- If the brand disappeared tomorrow, what would actually be lost?
- Who inside the company already lives the purpose? (If nobody, it isn't real.)

**Failure mode:** Purposewashing. Borrowing a noble cause for the campaign without
any business action behind it. Audiences are ruthless about this and the backlash
is permanent.

**Strategy on a Page shape:**
- The belief (what the brand stands for)
- The proof (what they do that costs them)
- The role in the audience's life
- The fight (who or what they're against)
- The provocation

## 8. Platform-led

**Lineage:** The big-platform tradition — "Just Do It", "Think Different", "Real
Beauty", "Find Your Greatness". A single unifying creative thought.

**When to use:**
- The brand needs a unifying creative idea that holds together across years and
  channels
- Brand has fragmented into too many tactical campaigns and needs a centre
- The brief is for an evergreen platform, not a single campaign
- There's a clear human truth the platform can hang on

**When *not* to use:**
- The brand isn't ready to commit to a long-term platform (it dies in year 2)
- The category moves so fast that any platform is obsolete in 18 months
- The "platform" is being used as decoration on top of unrelated tactical work

**Key questions:**
- If this brand could only say one thing for the next 5 years, what's the single
  thought?
- What's the idea that works in a manifesto AND on a button?
- What's the human truth this platform connects to?
- What does this platform unlock creatively that a tactical idea wouldn't?

**Failure mode:** Platforms that are actually tag-lines in disguise. A platform is
a creative *generative engine* — it should produce dozens of executions across
years. If you can only think of one ad for it, it's not a platform.

**Strategy on a Page shape:**
- The human truth
- The single thought
- The brand role
- The generative test (3 example expressions across formats)
- The provocation

## 9. Distinctive Brand Assets

**Lineage:** Jenni Romaniuk, *Building Distinctive Brand Assets*, Ehrenberg-Bass.

**When to use:**
- The brand is trying to build memorability over time, not differentiation
- The brand has assets that are inconsistent or weak today
- A rebrand is on the table and you need to know what *not* to throw away
- The brief is about long-term equity, not short-term sales

**When *not* to use:**
- The brand is too new to have assets to protect
- The brief is about meaning or positioning, not recognition
- DBAs are being used as a substitute for having any creative idea at all

**Key questions:**
- Which existing assets does the audience link to the brand without prompting?
- How fame and uniqueness do they score on (the Romaniuk grid)?
- Which assets are worth investing in / protecting / killing?
- What new asset could the brand build that doesn't exist in the category yet?

**Failure mode:** Treating DBAs as a *replacement* for strategy rather than a
component of it. "Just be more consistent with our colour" is not a strategy.

**Strategy on a Page shape:**
- The current asset audit (fame x uniqueness grid)
- Assets to protect / amplify
- Assets to retire
- New asset to build
- The provocation

## 10. Open *(let the research decide)*

When the user picks Open, do this:
1. Read the brief and the strategy type carefully.
2. Run the research (source-scrape + WebSearch).
3. Pick the 1-2 frameworks the evidence actually supports.
4. State *why* you picked them — one sentence each.
5. Generate the Strategy on a Page using the chosen framework's shape.

Don't run all 9 frameworks. That's strategy slop in a hat. The job of "Open" is
to make the framework choice *for* the user based on what the evidence shows.
