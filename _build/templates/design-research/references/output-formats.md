# Design Research Output Formats

One template per research mode. Always lead with the research summary — designers
scan first, read second.

---

## Mode A: Brand Deep-Dive

```markdown
# Brand Deep-Dive: {Brand Name}
**Researched by:** /creativestack:design-research — {date}
**Depth:** {Quick / Standard / Deep}

## Snapshot
{3-4 sentences. What they do, who they're for, where they sit in their category,
what they're known for. Specific, not generic. The opening sentence should be
something a stranger to the brand would actually learn from.}

## Visual System (current)

| Element | What they do | Notable |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Logo / mark | {description} | {what's distinctive or weak} |
| Typography | {primary + secondary typefaces, source if known} | {how they use it} |
| Colour palette | {core colours + use} | {how disciplined} |
| Layout | {grid / no grid / signature moves} | {what's recognisable} |
| Photography | {style — staged, documentary, abstract, none} | {who shoots / sources} |
| Illustration | {style or absent} | {if used, by whom} |
| Motion | {animation language if applicable} | {signature moves} |
| Voice | {tonal adjectives} | {hooks they reuse} |

**Where their identity is strongest:** {1-2 sentences}
**Where it's weakest or inconsistent:** {1-2 sentences}

## History
{2-4 paragraphs. Founding story, previous identities (with dates and designers if
known), major rebrands, why they happened, who did the work. Brand New is the
canonical source for rebrand archaeology — search by name.}

**Previous identities:**
- {year} — {description}, designed by {studio if known}
- {year} — {description}
- {year} — current system, designed by {studio if known}

## Content & cultural posture
{What do they post? Who do they collaborate with? What references do they lean on?
This tells you who they think they are — often very different from who they
actually are.}

## Strengths (what to protect)
- {Strength — specific}
- {Strength}
- {Strength}

## Gaps & risks (what's underweight or fragile)
- {Gap — specific}
- {Gap}
- {Gap}

## What we'd push if we worked on this brand
{2-4 bullets. Opinionated. The point of brand research is not just understanding —
it's having a point of view about where you'd take it.}

## Visual evidence
{Screenshots, links, captures from /source-scrape if run. Annotated with what
each one shows. Group by visual element if many.}

## Sources used
- {URL or source}
- {URL}
```

## Mode B: Visual Landscape

```markdown
# Visual Landscape: {Category / Brief}
**Researched by:** /creativestack:design-research — {date}

## Field map

| Brand | Role | Visual identity strength (1-10) | One-line read |
|-------|------|------|--------------|
| {brand} | Direct competitor | {n} | {what they look like} |
| {brand} | Direct competitor | {n} | {one-liner} |
| {brand} | Adjacent player | {n} | {one-liner} |
| {brand} | Wished-for peer | {n} | {one-liner} |

{8-15 brands. Score is a gut call, not science. The point of the score is to
surface which brands are worth dwelling on.}

## Category conventions (the things everyone is doing the same)

| Element | Convention | How tired it is |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Typography | {e.g., "geometric sans-serif, Inter or similar"} | {Fresh / Stable / Tired} |
| Colour | {e.g., "off-white background, single accent"} | {rating} |
| Photography | {e.g., "no people, abstract close-ups"} | {rating} |
| Layout | {e.g., "huge centered headline + small cap label"} | {rating} |
| Voice | {e.g., "wry, lowercase, 'we believe' language"} | {rating} |

**The category default look:** {1-2 sentences. If the client doesn't make a
choice, this is what they will look like.}

## Outliers worth studying
### {Brand name}
**What they're doing:** {1-2 sentences}
**Why it works:** {1 sentence}
**What's stealable:** {1 sentence — for the user's brief}

{Repeat for 3-5 outliers.}

## White space (genuinely unclaimed territory)
{1-2 paragraphs. The aesthetic territory no one in the category is occupying.
Be honest — most "white space" claims are wishful thinking. Only call it white
space if you can name *why* no one's there and *whether* the absence is meaningful.}

## Implications for the brief
- {Implication}
- {Implication}
- {Implication}

## Sources used
- {URLs}
```

## Mode C: Cultural & Creative Territories *(default mode — original output format)*

```markdown
# Design Research: {Project Name}
**Depth:** {Quick scan / Deep dive / Visual-heavy}
**Upstream data used:** {list skills that fed in, or "None — standalone research"}

## Research summary
{Short, opinionated paragraph. What themes keep emerging? Where is the most
promising creative territory? What should the team explore first? Which board has
the strongest potential? This is the most important section — designers scan it
first to decide if the rest is worth reading.}

---

## Research Boards

### Board 1: {Board Name — e.g., "Quiet Authority"}
**Mood:** {1-2 sentences describing the feeling — e.g., "Restrained confidence.
Lots of white space. Typography does the heavy lifting. Feels expensive without
trying."}

**References:**
1. **{Reference name}** — {type: campaign / identity / product / space / publication}
   {Why it's relevant: what *specific quality* connects it to this board and the
   brief. Aesthetic similarity isn't enough.}
   {Source + URL}
2. **{Reference}** — {type}
   {Relevance + source}
3. **{Reference}** — {type}
   {Relevance + source}
4. **{Reference}** — {type}
   {Relevance + source}

{4-6 references per board.}

**What this territory suggests for the brief:** {1-2 sentences — what creative
direction this board points toward}

**Where it could go wrong:** {1 sentence — the failure mode of this territory if
executed lazily. Forces the designer to know what to avoid.}

---

### Board 2: {Board Name}
{Same structure.}

### Board 3: {Board Name}
{Same structure.}

### Board 4: {Board Name — Deep dive and Visual-heavy only}
{Quick scan stops at 3 boards.}

---

## For the Creative Team
{References that push boundaries — less obvious, more provocative, the kind a senior
designer or CD would find exciting.}

### Cultural references
- **{Reference}** — {why it's relevant — feeling, not just look}
- **{Reference}** — {why}

### Design movements
- **{Movement}** — {specific connection — not a Wikipedia summary, an argument
  for why this movement matters here}

### Unexpected inspiration *(the wildcard section)*
- **{Inspiration}** — {the creative leap it suggests. A scientific concept, a
  ritual, a piece of writing, a material, a spatial experience.}

### Historical precedents *(Deep dive only)*
- **{Precedent}** — {what worked, what didn't, what's changed since}

### Counter-references *(what to avoid)*
- **{Reference}** — {the thing that's been overdone, why it would land flat for
  this brief, what it's become a cliché of}

---

## For the Client
{References the client will understand and respond to. Mainstream cultural
touchpoints, well-known brands, things they can show their board.}

### Recognisable brand references
- **{Brand}** — {what they did and why it's relevant} — {how it performed}

### Accessible cultural references
- **{Reference}** — {why it's relevant, framed in client language}

### The bridge
{1-2 sentences connecting the creative team references to the client references.
This is how you present ambitious work in terms the client can buy into.
e.g., "The creative team's Brutalist typography references translate into what
the client sees in {Brand}'s recent rebrand — bold, modern, confident. Same
territory, different entry point."}

---

## Adjacent industry examples
{Work from outside the client's category that solves a similar communication
problem. Best references rarely come from the same category.}

- **{Example}** — {what it does well that applies here}
- **{Example}** — {what it does well that applies here}

## Sources used
- {URLs and reference paths}
```

## Mode D: Craft Deep-Dive

```markdown
# Craft Deep-Dive: {Craft area — e.g., "Variable Type in Motion"}
**For project:** {project name}
**Researched by:** /creativestack:design-research — {date}

## What this craft is *(when it's contested)*
{1 paragraph. Define the boundaries of the craft and what it isn't. Useful when
the term is fuzzy — e.g., "type in motion" vs "kinetic typography" vs
"animated lettering" — these are different things and conflating them produces
weak work.}

## The canon *(recognised practitioners)*
- **{Name / Studio}** — {what they're known for, 1-2 sentences} — {link}
- **{Name / Studio}** — {what they're known for}
- {6-10 names — not the famous ones for the sake of it, the ones working
  designers actually study}

## The lineage *(where this craft came from)*
{2-3 paragraphs. Who influenced whom. Why this craft exists. The history matters
because copying without understanding lineage is the fastest way to look generic.}

## The contemporary edge *(who's doing something new right now)*
- **{Name / Studio}** — {what's new about it}
- **{Name / Studio}** — {what's new about it}
- {3-6 names — recent work, not catalogue famous}

## Failure modes *(what this craft looks like when it goes wrong)*
- **{Failure}** — {what it usually signals — laziness, lack of lineage knowledge,
  pattern misuse}
- **{Failure}** — {what it signals}
- {3-5 failure modes — often more instructive than the masters}

## Practical notes for the project
{2-4 bullets. Specific suggestions for how this craft research applies to the
brief. Tools, processes, references to revisit at execution stage.}

## Sources used
- {URLs}
```

## Mode E: Pattern Library

```markdown
# Pattern Research: {Pattern type — e.g., "Onboarding flows for B2B SaaS"}
**For project:** {project name}
**Researched by:** /creativestack:design-research — {date}

## Pattern type
{1-2 sentences. What kind of pattern this is, where it sits in a typical user
journey, what job it does.}

## Canonical examples *(8-15 across direct + adjacent products)*

| Product | Pattern variant | What's notable |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| {product} | {variant — e.g., "progressive disclosure, no upfront signup"} | {1 line} |
| {product} | {variant} | {1 line} |

## The convention *(what most products do)*
{1-2 paragraphs. The mental model users will bring to this pattern. Breaking
this without good reason makes the product feel broken, not innovative.}

## Deviations worth stealing
### {Product / pattern}
**What they did:** {1-2 sentences}
**Why it works:** {1 sentence}
**Stealable element:** {1 sentence}

{Repeat for 3-5 deviations.}

## Affordances to note
- **Microcopy:** {specific examples worth stealing — quote them verbatim}
- **Micro-interactions:** {specific moments — what triggers, how it animates,
  why it helps}
- **Motion:** {how the pattern uses motion to guide attention}
- **Empty / error / loading states:** {how the best examples handle them}

## Accessibility considerations
- {Specific consideration for this pattern type}
- {Specific consideration}

## Implications for the brief
- {Implication}
- {Implication}

## Sources used
- {URLs — Mobbin, Page Flows, etc.}
```

## Mode F: Audience Visual Diet

```markdown
# Audience Visual Diet: {Audience — narrowly defined}
**For project:** {project name}
**Researched by:** /creativestack:design-research — {date}

## The audience *(precisely defined)*
{1 short paragraph. Narrower is better. Not "Gen Z" — "UK Gen Z women into
thrifting and pole fitness". If the user gave a vague audience, name the
narrowing assumption you made.}

## Where they actually live online
- **{Platform}** — {specific places within it: hashtags, subreddits, accounts,
  servers, channels}
- **{Platform}** — {specific places}
- **{Platform}** — {specific places}

## What they consume
{What gets surfaced in their feeds, who they follow, what they save and screenshot.
Examples with context, not just lists.}

## What they make
{What this community produces themselves — memes, edits, fits, captions, art,
stitches. Often very different from what they consume passively.}

**The work usually needs to match what they make, not what they consume.**

## In-group signals *(belonging)*
- **{Signal}** — {what it signals and how it shows up visually}
- **{Signal}** — {how it shows up}
- {Specific visual cues, language, references, formats}

## No-go signals *(outsider giveaways)*
- **{Signal}** — {what reads as "an adult tried to do youth marketing"}
- **{Signal}** — {what to avoid}

## Implications for the brief
- {Implication}
- {Implication}
- {Implication}

## Sources used
- {URLs and observations}
```

## FigJam Output (any mode)

If Figma MCP is available, offer the FigJam board.

**Before generating any frame:** read `~/.creativestack/visual-style.md` if it
exists. Apply its typography, colour palette, spacing rules, and anti-slop
checklist to every frame. If it doesn't exist, fall back to the Editorial Modern
defaults and tell the user at the end:
> "Run `/creativestack:setup` to configure your house visual style for sharper
> research board layouts."

### Layout per mode

**Mode A — Brand Deep-Dive board:**
- Header strip: brand name + snapshot
- Visual system grid (one frame per element with screenshots)
- History timeline (horizontal)
- Strengths / Gaps split
- "What we'd push" callout
- Source list footer

**Mode B — Visual Landscape board:**
- Field map (axis chart, drag-positioned brand logos)
- Convention table
- Outliers row (3-5 frames)
- White space callout
- Implications footer

**Mode C — Cultural & Creative Territories board:**
- Section per board with reference cards
- Reference cards: image + name + relevance line
- "Creative Team" / "Client" split as separate sections
- Connector lines between related references across boards
- Colour coding by board theme

**Mode D — Craft Deep-Dive board:**
- Canon row (image + name)
- Lineage diagram (connector lines from influences to current)
- Contemporary edge row
- Failure modes callout strip

**Mode E — Pattern Library board:**
- Screen grid by pattern variant
- Convention summary callout
- Deviations row with annotations
- Affordances list

**Mode F — Audience Visual Diet board:**
- Audience definition header
- "Where they live" map
- "What they consume" row
- "What they make" row
- In-group / no-go split

After generating, walk through the anti-slop checklist in `visual-style.md`. Fix
anything that fails before showing the user.

If Figma MCP is **not** available, add at the end:
> "Want a visual research board? Install the Figma MCP server to generate FigJam
> boards directly from research output."
