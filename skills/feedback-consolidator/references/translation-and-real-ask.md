# Translation & The Real Ask

Two related jobs:

1. **Translation** — turning vague phrases into specific, actionable direction,
   using the client's own historical vocabulary when available
2. **The Real Ask** — finding the underlying concern the client keeps circling
   but hasn't stated directly

Both depend on the brain. Without `learnings.md § Feedback`, this is guesswork.
With it, it becomes precise.

---

## Part 1: Translation

### Step 1: Read the client vocab first

Before applying any generic translation, read `learnings.md § Feedback` for
this client. The Vocab subsection stores per-client translations:

```markdown
### Nike — Vocabulary
- **"bold"** → "brand-confident", not "louder". Source: Marcus uses this consistently.
- **"clean"** → "minimal but premium", not "low-contrast". Source: Priya, 3 projects.
- **"energy"** → movement/motion, not colour intensity. Source: brand team standard.
```

If the client has a known vocab, use it. The translation becomes:

> **Marcus said:** "Make it bolder"
> **Per Nike vocab:** He means "more brand-confident" — likely looking for
> stronger brand presence, not larger type or louder colour.

### Step 2: Apply generic translation patterns only if no brain data

Without a client vocab, the skill falls back to general patterns. These are
**starting hypotheses**, not confident translations. Always frame as
"possible meanings" and ask the client to confirm.

### Common vague phrases and their possible meanings

| Vague phrase | Possible meanings (context-dependent) | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| "Make it pop" | Increase contrast / Stronger focal point / Bolder colour / Bigger type hierarchy | Low — depends heavily on what they're reacting to |
| "More energy" | More movement / More colour saturation / More layered composition / More contrast | Low |
| "Too safe" | Not differentiated from category / Too conventional layout / Plays too much to the obvious / Feels like a template | Medium |
| "Cleaner" | More whitespace / Remove elements / Simpler hierarchy / Less decoration | Medium — context tells you which |
| "More premium" | More whitespace / Better type / Darker palette / Less busy / Craft detail visible | Medium |
| "Bolder" | Larger scale / Stronger colour / Bigger ambition / Stronger brand presence | Low |
| "Not quite there yet" | Unclear — usually means "I can't articulate what's wrong but I don't like it" | Very Low — ask follow-up |
| "I was expecting more" | Under-delivered on promised ambition / Too similar to existing work / Not distinctive enough | Medium |
| "Too busy" | Too many elements / Hierarchy unclear / Too much going on visually / Uncomfortable density | Medium |

| "Warmer" | Warmer palette / More approachable / More human voice / Softer edges | Medium |

### Step 3: Contextualise with the brief

After generating possible meanings, cross-reference against the brief. Which
interpretation is consistent with the brief's objectives?

> **Possible meanings:**
> - "Make it pop" = stronger focal point / bolder colour / bigger hierarchy
>
> **Brief context:** Brief objective is "distinctive in category" — which
> suggests the client is reacting to insufficient differentiation, not
> execution detail.
>
> **Most likely meaning:** The concept is too close to category norms. They
> want more differentiation, not more contrast. Focus on the idea before the
> execution.

This contextualised translation is the output — not a generic "make it pop
means increase contrast" dictionary.

### Step 4: Generate clarifying questions

For every Low-confidence translation, generate a clarifying question to send
back. The question should:

- Propose a specific interpretation, not ask an open question
- Offer the client an easy yes/no
- Close off ambiguity rather than opening more

**Bad:** "What do you mean by 'make it pop'?"
**Good:** "When you said 'make it pop', were you reacting to the concept
feeling too close to competitors, or to the visual execution specifically?"

### Step 5: Creative team brief (not bullet list)

Translate the feedback into a brief the creative team can actually work from.
Use `methodology.md` to match the agency's methodology language — this should
sound like a creative brief, not meeting notes.

Structure:

```markdown
## Creative Team Brief

**The feedback in one sentence:** {synthesis}

**What it means for the work:**
{2-3 sentences connecting the feedback to specific creative decisions. Use
the agency's methodology language — phases, frameworks, principles the team
already speaks.}

**Do:**
- {Specific action, tied to a principle}

**Don't:**
- {Specific action to avoid}

**Open for exploration:**
- {Area where the team has freedom}

**Open questions that need client resolution first:**
- {Item blocked on clarification}
```

The "Open questions" section prevents the team from starting work on blocked
items. Surface them clearly.

## Part 2: The Real Ask methodology

"The real ask" is the underlying concern the client keeps circling but hasn't
said directly. Finding it is the single highest-leverage insight in feedback
work.

The methodology uses **3 signals** across feedback rounds. Apply it to
Consolidate mode output when 2+ rounds exist.

### Signal 1: What they keep returning to (subconscious concern)

Look for themes that appear in 2+ rounds. Not the same exact words — the
same underlying topic.

Examples:
- Round 1: "feels corporate"
- Round 2: "too buttoned up"
- Round 3: "where's the personality"
- → Underlying concern: **the work feels stiff, they want more character**

The return signal is the strongest. If the client keeps coming back to it,
it's what they actually care about, even if they haven't articulated it.

### Signal 2: What they agree on without stating it (shared assumption)

Look at what **all stakeholders** agree on across all feedback, without
anyone having to defend it. This is the shared assumption — usually the
real success criterion they're measuring against.

Examples:
- Stakeholder A says "needs to feel premium"
- Stakeholder B says "not too cheap-looking"
- Stakeholder C says "quality first"
- → Shared assumption: **premium positioning is non-negotiable for everyone**

Shared assumptions are rarely stated in the brief because they're taken for
granted. Surfacing them explicitly gives the team a success criterion the
brief missed.

### Signal 3: What they're avoiding (elephant in the room)

Look for topics that appear in past meetings or the brief but are conspicuously
absent from the feedback. Silence on an important topic is a signal.

Examples:
- Brief said "must work in multiple markets"
- Feedback across 3 rounds mentions colour, type, messaging
- **Nobody has mentioned the multi-market dimension**
- → They're not thinking about it, OR they're avoiding it because they
  haven't made an internal decision yet

Avoidance signals are harder to detect but valuable. They predict where the
project will stumble later when the avoided topic becomes unavoidable.

### Combining the signals

The real ask is usually:

> **The [return signal] topic they can't articulate, driven by the [agreement
> signal] criterion they're measuring against, while avoiding the [avoidance
> signal] conversation that's really at stake.**

Example:

> "Across 3 rounds, the client keeps returning to wanting 'more personality'
> (return signal). All three stakeholders agree the work needs to feel
> 'premium' (agreement signal). Nobody has mentioned the Chinese market
> launch even though it's in the brief (avoidance signal).
>
> **The real ask:** They want a brand that feels premium AND warm — and
> they're worried the current direction is too Western for their Chinese
> launch, but haven't said so. The creative work isn't the problem; they're
> uncomfortable with a decision they haven't made yet."

### Real ask output format

```markdown
## The Real Ask

**Return signal:** {what they keep circling, across which rounds}
**Agreement signal:** {what all stakeholders silently agree on}
**Avoidance signal:** {what's conspicuously absent from the feedback}

**Synthesis (most likely real ask):** {one paragraph}

**What to do about it:**
- {Specific action — often a conversation, not a design change}
```

### When the real ask is not findable

Sometimes the signals don't converge. The client genuinely doesn't know what
they want yet, or the feedback is too sparse. In that case, say so:

> "The feedback doesn't show a clear pattern yet — rounds 1 and 2 focused on
> different things with no return theme. Recommend a single review call with
> all stakeholders before generating more work, so we can surface what they
> actually agree on."

Don't fabricate a real ask. The honest "we don't know yet" is more valuable
than a confident-sounding guess.

## Part 3: Capturing vocab learnings

When the skill detects a new client-specific meaning for a phrase, it should
offer to save it to `learnings.md § Feedback` during the compounding loop.

**Detection trigger:** The client uses a common phrase (from the vague phrases
table above) in a way that contradicts or refines the generic meaning, and
the clarification makes it clear what they actually meant.

Example capture:

> "I noticed Marcus used 'bold' today to mean 'brand-confident' — not
> 'louder colour' as the generic meaning suggests. This is his 2nd time
> using it this way (first was in the 2026-03-15 review per meeting log).
> Want me to save this as a vocab entry? Future translations for Nike will
> use his meaning automatically."

See `brain-feedback-schema.md` for the learnings.md write format.

### Vocab confidence thresholds

- **1 instance:** Don't save yet. Note as a candidate.
- **2 instances (same person, same meaning):** Save with Low confidence.
- **3-4 instances:** Medium confidence.
- **5+ instances:** High confidence. Apply with confidence in future runs.

Each time a saved vocab entry matches a new feedback item, increment its
instance count and update confidence.
