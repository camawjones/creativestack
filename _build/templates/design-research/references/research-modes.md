# Research Modes

Designers say "design research" to mean six very different things. Each mode needs a
different approach, different sources, and a different output. Pick the mode that
matches what the user actually needs — don't default to "cultural references" unless
that's genuinely what they asked for.

If the user is unsure, run `Cultural & creative territories` (the original mode) —
it's the broadest and the most useful for early-stage briefs.

---

## Mode A: Brand deep-dive

**Use when:** the user is researching a specific brand — usually a client, sometimes
a competitor or a hero brand. The goal is to understand the brand inside-out, not
just look at it.

**Research approach:**
1. **The brand's own archive first.** Their current site, social, brand book if
   public, annual reports, About / Story pages, founder origin, press kit, design
   system if exposed (e.g., GitHub, brand.x.com). Designers skip this constantly
   and it's the most valuable source.
2. **The brand's history.** Previous identity systems, rebrands, who designed them,
   why they changed. Brand New (Under Consideration) is the canonical source for
   this — search the archive.
3. **Their visual system.** Type, colour, layout, motion, photography, illustration,
   icon style, voice. Capture screenshots of the live site + key social posts.
4. **Their content & cultural posture.** What do they post about? Who do they
   collaborate with? What references do *they* lean on? This tells you who they
   think they are.
5. **The narrative around them.** Press, reviews, customer perception, controversies,
   anything that's shaped how they're seen.

**Curated sources:**
- Their own website + social + press kit (always start here)
- **Brand New** (Under Consideration) — rebrand archive, the single best source
- **Are.na** — search for the brand name; designers who follow them have curated boards
- **Fonts in Use** — what typefaces they actually use
- **Wayback Machine** — previous identity iterations
- **Companies House / SEC filings** — for B2B / financial context (not visual but
  shapes the work)
- **Glassdoor / LinkedIn** — culture and team signals
- **Brand archives** if available — Pentagram, Wolff Olins, COLLINS, Manual,
  Mother Design, Order, &Walsh case studies if they did the work
- **D&AD / The Brand Identity / Brand Identity Grid / It's Nice That** — coverage

**Output:** "Brand Deep-Dive" template (see output-formats.md). One document, not
research boards. Sections: snapshot, visual system, history, content posture,
strengths, gaps & risks, what we'd push if we worked on this brand.

## Mode B: Visual landscape (category / competitive)

**Use when:** the user wants to see the visual conventions of a category or
competitive set before designing for it. Less about specific brands than about the
*shape* of the category.

**Research approach:**
1. **Map the field.** 8–15 brands across direct competitors, adjacent players, and
   "the brand the client wishes they were."
2. **Pattern-spot relentlessly.** What's everyone doing the same? Type choices,
   colour, layout, photography style, voice. The shared moves are the category
   default — that's what the client will look like if they don't make a choice.
3. **Find the outliers.** Who's breaking the pattern, and is it working?
4. **Identify the white space.** What aesthetic territory is genuinely unclaimed?
5. **Score each brand on visual identity strength** if useful (1–10, with one-line
   reason).

**Curated sources:**
- The brands' own sites + social
- **Brand New** for any of them with recent rebrands
- **Land-book**, **Httpster**, **godly.website**, **SiteInspire** — for digital
- **Awwwards** for category filtering
- **Fonts in Use** for shared type signals
- **Are.na** for designer-curated channel boards on the category

**Output:** "Visual Landscape" template (see output-formats.md). Includes a
landscape map (axis chart), pattern table, white space analysis, and 3–5 outlier
case studies with what they're doing right.

If the user has run `/competitor-audit` already, build on it — don't
duplicate the strategic analysis. This mode is the visual layer underneath.

## Mode C: Cultural & creative territories *(default mode — the original strength)*

**Use when:** the brief is open enough to explore creative direction. The user wants
themed research boards that point to distinct creative territories rather than a
single answer.

**Research approach:**
1. Read the brief, extract its emotional and aesthetic coordinates.
2. Build 3–4 *distinct* creative territories — not variations on the same theme.
3. For each territory, gather references across disciplines (graphic, motion,
   photography, fashion, architecture, music, film, subcultures).
4. Reach beyond design press into adjacent worlds. The best references rarely come
   from It's Nice That.
5. Make every reference earn its place — what *specific quality* connects it to
   this brief? Aesthetic similarity isn't enough.

**Curated sources:**
- **Are.na** — non-negotiable for serious cultural research; search by tag, follow
  designers who curate the territory
- **Cosmos.so** — newer image-first browsing
- **It's Nice That**, **Dezeen**, **Sidetracked**, **The Brand Identity**,
  **Brand New** — design press
- **Fonts in Use**, **Typewolf**, **Typographica** — typography
- **AGI Open**, **D&AD annual archive**, **Design Observer** — heritage and craft
- **V&A**, **Cooper Hewitt**, **MoMA**, **Centre Pompidou** — museum collections
  for historical precedent
- **Wallpaper\***, **Apartamento**, **032c**, **Toilet Paper** — adjacent culture
- **Vimeo Staff Picks**, **Motionographer**, **STASH** — motion
- **AnOther**, **i-D**, **SSENSE**, **Hypebeast** — fashion / cultural commentary
- **Pitchfork**, **The Quietus**, **Resident Advisor** — music & subculture
- **ArchDaily**, **Divisare**, **Frieze**, **e-flux** — architecture & art

**Output:** Themed research boards (3–4) + Cultural References + Design Movements
+ Unexpected Inspiration + dual-audience split (Creative Team / Client). This is
the mode the original skill was built around — keep using its full output format.

## Mode D: Craft deep-dive

**Use when:** the user wants to research a specific craft area in depth —
typography, illustration, motion, photography, photography styling, print finishing,
3D, type-in-motion, etc. The audience is the designer alone, not the client.

**Research approach:**
1. **Establish the canon.** Who are the recognised practitioners in this craft? Not
   just the famous ones — the ones working designers actually study.
2. **Trace the lineage.** Where did this craft come from? Who influenced whom?
   Lineage matters because copying without understanding lineage is the fastest way
   to look generic.
3. **Find the contemporary edge.** Who's doing something genuinely new in this
   craft right now?
4. **Document the technique** when relevant — tools, processes, materials,
   workflows.
5. **Show the failure modes.** What does this craft look like when it goes wrong?
   Often more instructive than the masters.

**Curated sources by craft:**

**Typography:**
- Fonts in Use, Typographica, Typewolf, Klim Type Foundry blog, Commercial Type,
  Lineto, Dinamo, Grilli Type, ABC Dinamo, Pangram Pangram, Future Fonts
- The collected works of Massimo Vignelli, Wim Crouwel, Karel Martens, Erik
  Spiekermann, Jonathan Hoefler — for lineage

**Illustration:**
- It's Nice That illustration, AOI (Association of Illustrators), Folioart,
  Pictoright, Booooooom, Levine Leavitt, Handsome Frank
- Specific contemporary names that change every 18 months — surface 5–10 current
  ones by searching It's Nice That and Are.na

**Motion / type in motion:**
- Motionographer, Vimeo Staff Picks (Animation), STASH magazine, Behance Motion,
  Grand Matter, Buck, Manvsmachine, Animade, Tendril, Golden Wolf — studio archives
- Type-in-motion specifically: type-in-motion supercut search, Maan Studio, Animography

**Photography / image-making:**
- Booooooom, IGNANT, AnOther, i-D, Self Service, Re-edition Magazine, Magnum,
  Aperture, Itsalovestory, Offline Magazine

**Print / finishing / production:**
- Counter-Print, Studio Mut archive, Publication Studio, Print magazine archive,
  Atelier Marge Design, paper merchants' sample books

**3D / CGI:**
- Vimeo CGI, Behance 3D, Cinema 4D community, Active Theory, Field Studio, Fons
  Schiedon, Universal Everything, Onformative

**Output:** "Craft Deep-Dive" template (see output-formats.md) — Canon, Lineage,
Contemporary edge, Failure modes, Practical notes for the project.

## Mode E: Pattern research (UI / UX / interaction)

**Use when:** the user is designing a digital product and wants to research
interaction patterns, screen archetypes, conventions, and break opportunities.
Not visual identity research — *interaction* research.

**Research approach:**
1. **Identify the pattern type.** Onboarding, empty states, settings, feed, search,
   filtering, dashboards, paywalls, account creation, etc.
2. **Pull 8–15 examples across direct and adjacent products.** Adjacent matters —
   the best banking app pattern often comes from a fitness app.
3. **Look for the convention.** What's everyone doing the same? That's the user's
   mental model.
4. **Find the deviations that work.** Who broke the pattern, why, and did it
   actually improve the experience?
5. **Note the affordances.** Specific microcopy, micro-interactions, motion,
   accessibility moves.

**Curated sources:**
- **Mobbin** — non-negotiable; the deepest searchable mobile pattern library
- **Page Flows**, **Refero**, **UI Sources**, **Screenlane** — flows and screens
- **Land-book**, **godly.website**, **httpster**, **SiteInspire**, **Awwwards** —
  marketing site patterns
- **Pttrns**, **Collect UI**, **SaaS Pages** — pattern-specific
- **Apple HIG**, **Material Design**, **Spectrum (Adobe)**, **Polaris (Shopify)**,
  **Atlassian Design System** — system principles
- **Smart Interface Design Patterns** (Vitaly Friedman), **Refactoring UI**,
  **Nielsen Norman Group** articles for the why behind patterns

**Output:** "Pattern Library" template (see output-formats.md) — pattern type,
canonical examples, convention summary, deviations worth stealing, affordances to
note, accessibility considerations.

## Mode F: Audience visual diet

**Use when:** the user needs to understand what the *audience* actually looks at,
not what designers think they should look at. Critical for work that has to feel
native to a specific subculture, generation, or community.

**Research approach:**
1. **Identify the audience precisely.** Not "Gen Z" — "UK Gen Z women into
   thrifting and pole fitness". The narrower the better.
2. **Find where they actually live online.** Specific subreddits, TikTok hashtags,
   Discord servers, Twitch streamers, Instagram accounts, YouTube creators, niche
   forums. Not the platforms — the *places within them*.
3. **Capture what they share with each other.** Memes, screenshots, reactions,
   in-jokes, the visual language of the community.
4. **Note the in-group signals.** What signals belonging? What signals being an
   outsider? These are the highest-stakes details for native-feeling work.
5. **Distinguish what they consume from what they make.** They're often very
   different — and the design work usually needs to match what they make, not what
   they consume passively.

**Curated sources:**
- The audience's own platforms — TikTok hashtag pages, subreddit top-of-month,
  Discord server screenshots (with permission), Twitch chat, Instagram top
  followed accounts within the niche
- **Know Your Meme** — for visual conventions and lineage
- **TrendTok**, **Glimpse**, **Exploding Topics** — for emerging signals
- **Substack newsletters** in the niche — qualitative voice
- **Are.na boards** curated by people in the audience, not designers
- **Patreon top creators** in the niche

**Output:** "Audience Visual Diet" template (see output-formats.md) — the
audience-as-defined, where they live, what they consume, what they make, in-group
signals, no-go signals, implications for the design.

## Mode picker

Don't ask for the mode in jargon. Ask in plain English:

> "What kind of research do you need?
> - **Brand deep-dive** — research a specific brand inside-out (often a client)
> - **Visual landscape** — see the visual conventions of a category before
>   designing for it
> - **Cultural & creative territories** — open exploration, themed research
>   boards (default for early-stage briefs)
> - **Craft deep-dive** — go deep on a specific craft (type, illustration,
>   motion, etc.)
> - **Pattern research** — UI/UX patterns for digital product work
> - **Audience visual diet** — what does the audience actually look at?"

If the user is unsure, default to **Cultural & creative territories**. Combinations
are fine — `Brand deep-dive + Visual landscape` is a common pairing for a rebrand
pitch.
