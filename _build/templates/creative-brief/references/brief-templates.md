# Brief Templates by Project Type

Templates for 11 common project types. Each includes:

- **Typical deliverables** — the list a brief of this type usually covers
- **Common constraints** — what usually limits or shapes the work
- **Common gaps** — where briefs of this type tend to be weak (from industry experience + `learnings.md` overlay when available)
- **Type-specific pressure tests** — checks that matter more for this type than others
- **Starter tension patterns** — common tension archetypes for this project type to help the user articulate theirs

The skill uses the template as a starting scaffold, not a prescription. The
final brief should always be shaped to the specific situation. Apply
`learnings.md` patterns on top of the template where available.

## Rebrand

**Typical deliverables:**
- Brand strategy (positioning, purpose, values)
- Visual identity (logo, colour, typography, system)
- Brand guidelines
- Key applications (web, signage, stationery, environment)
- Rollout plan
- Internal launch materials

**Common constraints:**
- Existing brand equity to protect (logo recognition, colour associations, customer familiarity)
- Internal stakeholder alignment (the brand belongs to everyone inside the org)
- Legal/trademark continuity
- Budget and timeline for rollout (often the most expensive phase)
- Multi-market considerations

**Common gaps:**
- Success criteria are vague ("modernise" is not a criterion)
- Internal audience overlooked (brand rollout fails without employee buy-in)
- Rollout timeline missing
- Equity-protection decisions deferred ("we'll keep what works" without specifying what works)
- No distinction between strategic refresh and full rebrand

**Type-specific pressure tests:**
- Is this a refresh or a rebrand? (The delta affects cost 5-10×)
- Which equity elements are sacred?
- Who owns the decision internally — and is that person in the brief process?
- Is the rollout budget separated from the design budget?

**Starter tension patterns:**
- "The brand's perception is stuck in [past position] but the business has moved to [new position]"
- "We're seen as [category norm] but we need to be seen as [differentiated position]"
- "Our internal culture says [one thing] but our external brand says [different thing]"

## Campaign

**Typical deliverables:**
- Creative concept + big idea
- Key visuals across channels
- Copy (headlines, long-form, taglines)
- Media assets by channel spec
- Production plan
- Measurement framework

**Common constraints:**
- Media plan dates (hard deadlines, non-negotiable)
- Channel specs and platform rules
- Budget splits between production and media
- Approval layers (especially for regulated categories)
- Legal sign-off on claims

**Common gaps:**
- Measurement framework missing ("how will we know it worked?")
- Audience insight too broad ("young urban professionals" is not an insight)
- No competitive context (the campaign will appear in a media environment, not in isolation)
- The single-minded thought is actually a spec ("we need a TV ad" is not a thought)
- Desired response absent — what should the audience do, feel, believe after seeing this?

**Type-specific pressure tests:**
- What's the one thing we want people to think, feel, or do differently?
- Is the insight actually an insight, or is it a demographic?
- Can the concept extend across the media plan, or is it a single-format idea?
- What does success look like — and what would failure look like?

**Starter tension patterns:**
- "Our audience doesn't think about the category at all — how do we make them care?"
- "Our audience thinks they already know us — how do we surprise them?"
- "The category is dominated by [dominant position] — how do we own a different space?"

## Digital Product

**Typical deliverables:**
- UX strategy and information architecture
- UI design and component library
- Interactive prototype
- Design system documentation
- Developer handoff (tokens, specs, assets)
- Post-launch iteration plan

**Common constraints:**
- Tech stack (existing backend, frameworks, CMS)
- Accessibility requirements (legal in many markets)
- Existing design system (to extend or replace)
- Launch date (often tied to marketing moment)
- Content realities (placeholder copy hides real content problems)

**Common gaps:**
- User research depth ("we talked to some users" ≠ research)
- Content strategy ignored ("content will be provided")
- Post-launch iteration plan absent (launch is the middle, not the end)
- No definition of success beyond shipping
- Accessibility treated as QA checklist rather than design input

**Type-specific pressure tests:**
- Is the brief describing a product or a website? (They're not the same.)
- Who's the real user — is it the buyer or the end-user?
- What's the success metric beyond launch — retention, conversion, task completion?
- Is there a real content person on the project, or is "content TBD"?

**Starter tension patterns:**
- "Users expect [category norm] but the business needs [different behaviour]"
- "The brand is premium but the current experience is confusing and cheap"
- "The tech stack forces [constraint] but the user need requires [different approach]"

## Identity System

**Typical deliverables:**
- Master logo system (including variations, lock-ups, clear space)
- Colour palette (primary, secondary, functional, accessible pairs)
- Typography system (display, body, hierarchy)
- Iconography / graphic language
- Photography direction
- Guidelines document
- Core toolkit / templates

**Common constraints:**
- Multi-brand architecture (house brands, sub-brands, endorsed brands)
- Existing parent or sibling brands
- Global / local flexibility (what's locked, what flexes by market)
- Application environment (web, print, signage, motion, merch)
- Governance model (who approves future uses)

**Common gaps:**
- Application scope unclear (is this for web only, or everything?)
- Motion / digital extensions undefined
- Governance model not specified (who holds the brand after handoff?)
- Photography direction mentioned but no actual direction provided
- No demonstration of the system stretching (briefs under-test system flexibility)

**Type-specific pressure tests:**
- How does the system handle motion, digital, and photography — not just logo and colour?
- Where does the system flex and where does it lock?
- Who'll govern the system after we hand it over?
- Have we accounted for the edge cases (co-branding, partnerships, legal mentions)?

**Starter tension patterns:**
- "The brand has ambition but the current identity can't scale"
- "We're a modern company wearing an identity from [era]"
- "The system needs to work for [craft-focused use case] AND [mass-market use case]"

## Naming

**Typical deliverables:**
- Name candidates (usually 3-10 finalists)
- Naming rationale for each
- Linguistic check (unintended meaning in target markets)
- Trademark screening (preliminary — not legal clearance)
- Domain availability
- Recommendation and approach for final decision

**Common constraints:**
- Legal availability (the biggest filter — most names fail here)
- Domain availability
- Cultural sensitivity across target markets
- Existing portfolio (can't clash with other names in the house)
- Linguistic pronunciation (if multi-market)

**Common gaps:**
- Evaluation criteria not defined (what makes a name "good"?)
- Stakeholder alignment on direction (some people want descriptive, others want abstract — needs to be locked early)
- Timeline for legal clearance (legal screening takes weeks, not days)
- No clear decision-making process (naming dies in committee more than any other deliverable)

**Type-specific pressure tests:**
- What are the criteria — memorability, pronounceability, meaning, ownability, legal clearance? Rank them.
- Who decides? (Naming decisions made by committee always produce weak names.)
- How much time is allocated to legal and linguistic checks?
- What's the tolerance for abstract vs descriptive names?

**Starter tension patterns:**
- "We need a name that's distinctive AND safe — but those pull opposite ways"
- "The name needs to work for [current business] and [future ambition]"
- "Descriptive says what we do, abstract says what we are — which matters more?"

## Packaging

**Typical deliverables:**
- Packaging strategy and structural brief
- Primary pack design (front, back, side panels)
- Secondary pack / outer packaging
- Range architecture (if a portfolio)
- Dielines and production specs
- Shelf mockups
- Print specifications

**Common constraints:**
- Structural limits (existing bottle, carton, can — can we change it?)
- Shelf context (where the pack lives in the retail environment)
- Production methods and costs (printing techniques, substrates)
- Regulatory requirements (ingredients, nutrition, hazard labels, language)
- Sustainability requirements

**Common gaps:**
- Shelf photography or IRL context absent (designed on white backgrounds, fails in store)
- Range architecture thinking — pack 1 looks great, pack 2 doesn't work with it
- Production realities ignored until too late (ink limitations, substrate compatibility)
- Hierarchy decisions deferred (what's the most important thing on the front?)
- No usability / readability test

**Type-specific pressure tests:**
- Have we tested this in a shelf environment?
- Can the design scale across the range?
- Are we designing for the producer's capabilities?
- What's the hierarchy — brand, product, benefit, flavour?

**Starter tension patterns:**
- "We need to stand out on shelf AND fit the brand system"
- "Premium positioning vs production constraints"
- "Legibility at arm's length vs craft detail up close"

## Retainer

**Typical deliverables:**
- Scope of retainer (hours, types of work included, types excluded)
- Monthly deliverable cadence
- Governance model (who requests work, who approves, who measures)
- Quarterly review framework
- Escalation path for ad-hoc or out-of-scope requests

**Common constraints:**
- Hours per month (the primary cost lever)
- Types of work included / excluded (the primary scope lever)
- Response time expectations
- Team availability (overlap with project work)
- Reporting frequency

**Common gaps:**
- No clear "in scope" vs "out of scope" definition (the biggest source of retainer disputes)
- No escalation path for big requests that arrive inside a retainer
- No measurement framework (retainers go quiet and then cancel because nobody tracked value)
- No governance cadence (who reviews the retainer each quarter?)
- Team continuity assumed but not guaranteed

**Type-specific pressure tests:**
- What happens when a big request arrives? (Clear answer, not "we'll figure it out.")
- How does the client know they're getting value each month?
- What's the exit / review clause?
- Who's the single point of contact both ways?

**Starter tension patterns:**
- "Client wants flexibility, agency needs predictability"
- "Retainers should feel collaborative, not transactional"
- "Monthly output should feel like ongoing value, not a quota"

## Pitch Brief (agency-initiated)

**Use case:** The agency is preparing for a pitch. There's no client brief
yet — the brief is what the agency thinks the prospect *should* be asking for.

**Typical deliverables:**
- Pitch concept and big idea
- Supporting case studies and positioning
- Scope proposal (what the engagement would include)
- Credentials tailored to the prospect
- The killer hook (from `pitch-research` if available)

**Common constraints:**
- Pitch deadline (hard, external)
- Available pitch team capacity (`resource-conflict` data)
- Credentials that fit (case-studies.md)
- The prospect's apparent constraints (inferred from public research)
- Internal budget for pitch effort (unpaid work has a cost)

**Common gaps:**
- Assumes too much about the prospect's real situation
- Creative ambition not matched to prospect's likely risk tolerance
- Credentials chosen by proximity, not relevance
- No killer hook — the pitch opens with credentials
- Pitch team not actually cast

**Type-specific pressure tests:**
- Is there a real pitch or are we guessing? (Read `prospects/{slug}.md` if it exists.)
- Does the killer hook from the pitch research actually drive the brief?
- Is the creative ambition defensible given the decision-maker's likely risk tolerance?
- What's the agency's ROI math on winning vs losing?

**Starter tension patterns:**
- "The prospect says X, our research says Y — we should bet on Y"
- "The obvious pitch is [generic], the brave pitch is [specific]"
- "We're not the biggest — we need to be the most interesting"

**From-pitch mode:** If a prospect dossier exists (`prospects/{slug}.md`) and
the user picks **From pitch** mode, pre-populate:
- Background ← prospect dossier "Brand Snapshot"
- Category context ← prospect dossier "Competitor Landscape" + "Trend Position"
- Audience ← prospect dossier "Decision-Maker Profiles" (for the pitch audience)
- Angles ← prospect dossier "Angles In — Ranked by Fit"
- The single-minded thought ← prospect dossier "Killer Hook"
- Warm path ← prospect dossier "Warm Path In"

## Phase Brief

**Use case:** A multi-phase engagement where each phase has its own brief.
This template is for briefing phase 2+ of an existing project.

**Typical deliverables:**
- Phase-specific deliverables (from the overall SOW)
- Dependencies from previous phase
- Handoffs to next phase

**Common constraints:**
- What was agreed in the previous phase (non-negotiable)
- What was deferred from previous phase (still owed)
- Team continuity (same people or new?)
- Budget envelope for this phase

**Common gaps:**
- Assumes the master brief is still current (it's usually not)
- Doesn't acknowledge learnings from previous phase
- Team continuity assumptions unchecked
- Phase success criteria unclear

**Type-specific pressure tests:**
- What changed since the master brief was written?
- What did we learn in the previous phase that should reshape this one?
- Is the phase success criteria the same as the master criteria, or different?
- Are we rebrief-ing enough, or just updating?

**Starter tension patterns:**
- "We learned X in phase 1 — how does that change phase 2?"
- "The original plan said Y — but the project now needs Z"

## Research / Strategy Brief

**Use case:** The deliverable is insight, not design. Strategy projects,
audience research, brand audits, positioning work.

**Typical deliverables:**
- Research plan and methodology
- Findings and insights report
- Strategic recommendations
- Positioning / territory options
- Workshop materials
- Brand platform (if applicable)

**Common constraints:**
- Access to stakeholders and customers (the limiting factor for research depth)
- Time for primary research
- Confidentiality / NDA requirements
- Output format expectations (deck, doc, workshop, all three)
- Pre-existing research to build on

**Common gaps:**
- "Research" defined as desk research only
- No clear decision the research will inform
- No success criteria ("we'll know when we see it")
- Output format expectations misaligned
- Stakeholder access assumed but not confirmed

**Type-specific pressure tests:**
- What decision will this research inform? (If none, don't do the research.)
- Are we doing primary research, secondary, or both?
- Who needs to be interviewed — and do we have access?
- What does the output enable next?

**Starter tension patterns:**
- "We have the data but lack the framework"
- "Everyone has opinions, nobody has evidence"
- "We know our customer but not our non-customer"

## Content / Editorial

**Use case:** Ongoing or campaign-based content production — video, editorial,
social content, long-form, newsletters.

**Typical deliverables:**
- Content strategy and pillars
- Content calendar
- Asset specs by format
- Editorial guidelines
- Workflow and production model
- Measurement framework

**Common constraints:**
- Cadence (daily, weekly, monthly)
- Platform specs (each platform has its own format rules)
- SEO / discoverability requirements
- Brand voice and guidelines
- Approval speed (fast-moving content needs fast approval)

**Common gaps:**
- No distinction between always-on and campaign content
- Production model unspecified (who makes what)
- Voice guidelines vague ("be authentic")
- Measurement = vanity metrics
- Approval process not modelled against cadence requirements

**Type-specific pressure tests:**
- Can the production model actually sustain the cadence?
- Is the approval process faster than the publishing cadence?
- What's the content actually for — awareness, consideration, conversion, loyalty?
- Is there a "kill criteria" — at what metric do we stop making this?

**Starter tension patterns:**
- "We need volume AND quality"
- "The audience has infinite content choices — why watch ours?"
- "Platforms change monthly, our process changes yearly"

## Template selection flow

1. **User picks a type** from the select list OR asks the skill to infer from the input content
2. **Skill reads the matching template section**
3. **Skill overlays `learnings.md` patterns** for this client or this project type
4. **Skill populates the brief scaffold** with the template defaults, adjusted for actual input
5. **Skill flags the type-specific common gaps** as Open Questions if they aren't covered by the input
6. **Skill runs type-specific pressure tests** after the brief is drafted and surfaces any that fail

## When no template fits

If the user picks "Other" or the inferred type doesn't match any template,
use a generic creative brief scaffold with no pre-populated deliverables or
constraints. Ask the user once to describe the nature of the work, then
proceed without template defaults.

## Inferring type from input

Heuristics for inferring:
- Mentions of "rebrand", "refresh", "new identity" → Rebrand or Identity
- Mentions of "campaign", "launch", "advert" → Campaign
- Mentions of "website", "app", "product", "user experience" → Digital Product
- Mentions of "name", "naming", "renaming" → Naming
- Mentions of "packaging", "pack", "dieline" → Packaging
- Mentions of "retainer", "ongoing", "monthly hours" → Retainer
- Mentions of "pitch", "RFP", "credentials", "speculative" → Pitch Brief
- Mentions of "phase 2", "next phase", "continuing" → Phase Brief
- Mentions of "research", "insight", "strategy", "audit", "positioning" → Research/Strategy
- Mentions of "content", "editorial", "social", "newsletter" → Content/Editorial

If inference fails, ask the user rather than guessing.
