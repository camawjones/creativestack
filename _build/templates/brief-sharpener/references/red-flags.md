# Red Flag Pattern Library

Patterns that repeatedly show up in client briefs and reliably
predict project pain. Not every flag kills a project — but every
flag should be surfaced, not silently accepted. Read this every
time, flag what's present, and let the creative team decide how to
respond with full information.

Organised by category. Each pattern has **what it looks like**,
**why it matters**, and **how to surface it**.

---

## Budget & commercial red flags

### "No budget constraints"
- **Looks like:** "Let creative lead, we'll find the money" / "Don't worry about cost at this stage"
- **Why it matters:** Either the client has no authority to commit budget (most common), or they genuinely don't know what the work should cost (second most common), or the scope is about to explode in rounds 2-3. All three are project-killers.
- **How to surface:** "For scoping purposes we work to a budget range — what's the ballpark the team should plan against? Even a broad range (£20k / £50k / £100k / £250k+) helps us match ambition to reality."

### Budget-scope mismatch
- **Looks like:** 15 deliverables in the brief, £12k stated budget
- **Why it matters:** The client either doesn't know what things cost (fixable with education) or expects a discount (unfixable without a conversation)
- **How to surface:** Cite specific items against typical ranges from `rate-card.md` if available. Present the gap as neutral data, not a complaint: "The deliverable list typically scopes to £{X}-{Y} at our rate card. Can we talk about priorities vs budget?"

### Budget unstated, scope maximalist
- **Looks like:** Long deliverable list, no budget, no timeline
- **Why it matters:** Classic "dream brief" — the client has put their wishlist in writing but not done the hard thinking about trade-offs
- **How to surface:** Force a priority call: "If we could only do 3 of these in phase 1, which 3?"

### Pay-per-performance / revenue share hints
- **Looks like:** "Bonus if we hit the numbers" / "We'd love to share upside" / "Performance-based fee"
- **Why it matters:** Usually means the client doesn't have the budget for the work. Rarely a real upside conversation.
- **How to surface:** Ask directly if the base fee covers the work as scoped, separately from any upside structure.

## Scope & timeline red flags

### Timeline impossibilities
- **Looks like:** "Launch in 3 weeks" on a full brand identity, "6-week rebrand" at scale, "Ready for Monday" on a deliverable that takes 2 weeks of concept work
- **Why it matters:** Unrealistic timeline = either shortcut work the team doesn't want to put its name on, or a broken trust relationship when it slips
- **How to surface:** Don't fight the date — surface the sequence: "For this scope, concept → refine → apply → launch is typically {X} weeks. Which stage should we compress, and what's the trade-off cost?"

### "Evolution not revolution"
- **Looks like:** "It's not a rebrand, just a refresh" paired with deliverables that read as a full rebrand
- **Why it matters:** Either the client is underestimating scope, or the word "rebrand" is politically charged internally. Either way the team needs to know which it is before starting.
- **How to surface:** "Are we avoiding the word 'rebrand' because it's a political term internally, or because you genuinely want lighter-touch work? The difference changes the team and scope significantly."

### Scope creep pre-loaded
- **Looks like:** "And we might also need X" / "Phase 2 could include..." / "It'd be great if you could also..."
- **Why it matters:** These asides always become deliverables. Scoping them out now is easier than descoping them later.
- **How to surface:** Pull every "might" and "could" into an Assumptions section in the sharpened brief with explicit "In / Out" of phase 1.

### Timeline shorter than approval process
- **Looks like:** 4-week timeline, 6-stakeholder approval process
- **Why it matters:** Brief is mathematically impossible. The team will burn out in the middle.
- **How to surface:** Count the stakeholder layers, count the approval days, subtract from the timeline. Show the math.

## Audience & strategy red flags

### Audience is everyone
- **Looks like:** "Primary: B2B decision-makers. Secondary: general public. Tertiary: employees." / "Anyone who could benefit"
- **Why it matters:** A brief targeted at everyone is targeted at no one. Creative work that tries to talk to everyone becomes wallpaper.
- **How to surface:** Force the primary: "If we had to pick one person this work needs to reach above all others, who?"

### Audience is a persona doc
- **Looks like:** Full persona with name, age, hobbies, "Sarah, 34, loves yoga and coffee"
- **Why it matters:** Personas are not audiences — they're stock photography with a CV. They're also often made up by marketing without research.
- **How to surface:** Ask what the audience *does* and *wants*, not who they *are*. Layer 1 and Layer 4 from the 4-layer audience check.

### "The vision is to be like Apple"
- **Looks like:** Client wants the brand to be "like Apple" / "like Nike" / "like Patagonia"
- **Why it matters:** Either the client doesn't know what to ask for (most common) or they genuinely mean it (and the brief needs a budget conversation)
- **How to surface:** Reflect back *which specific thing* about the reference brand: "When you say 'like Apple', do you mean the minimalist visual language, the brand premium, the product experience, or the cultural position? Each implies a very different project."

## Tone & contradiction red flags

### Classic tone contradictions
- "Premium but accessible"
- "Bold but safe"
- "Fun but serious"
- "Modern but timeless"
- "Innovative but trusted"
- "Disruptive but safe"
- "Simple but comprehensive"

**Why it matters:** These aren't always wrong — sometimes there's a real tension the brand is trying to hold. But they're almost always the first thing creatives have to resolve, and the resolution is political, not creative.

**How to surface:** Name the contradiction explicitly with [CONTRADICTION] and reflect: "Premium and accessible can coexist, but they pull the work in different directions. Which is the priority if we have to choose — premium in craft with accessibility in pricing/voice, or accessible everywhere and premium only on hero moments?"

### "We don't want to constrain creativity"
- **Looks like:** "Use the brief as a guide, we trust the team" / "Don't feel restricted by these notes" / "We want to be wowed"
- **Why it matters:** Briefs that say this *always* end in 6 rounds of "we're not quite there yet" feedback. The client has constraints — they just haven't said them out loud. Usually because naming them feels un-creative to them.
- **How to surface:** "Completely get that — one question we ask every client who says this: what's the *one thing* you definitely don't want this work to be? That helps us stay in the right neighbourhood."

## Process & relationship red flags

### The phantom decision-maker
- **Looks like:** Brief mentions a CEO or founder who hasn't been in any of the calls
- **Why it matters:** The phantom will show up in round 3 with a completely different view. All prior work becomes wasted effort.
- **How to surface:** "Will {name} be in the kickoff / concept review? The earlier they're in the room, the less rework later."

### Committee approval with no decision-maker
- **Looks like:** "The exec team will review" / "We'll align internally and come back"
- **Why it matters:** Committees don't make decisions — individuals do. If no single person owns the decision, the work will be designed by compromise.
- **How to surface:** Ask directly: "Who has the final call if the exec team disagrees?"

### "Let us know what you need"
- **Looks like:** Minimal brief with offer to provide more if asked
- **Why it matters:** The client is offloading the briefing work to the agency — fine if you're charging for it, a problem if you're not
- **How to surface:** Redirect to `/creative-brief` From scratch mode as a paid workshop.

### Previous agency mention
- **Looks like:** "We worked with {agency} on this before" / "The last team didn't quite get it"
- **Why it matters:** If there was a prior agency, understanding *why it ended* is the single most predictive signal for whether this relationship will work
- **How to surface:** Ask directly but without loading: "What was working with {agency} like? What would you want us to do differently?"

### "We've already done the thinking"
- **Looks like:** Brief arrives with a pre-decided creative direction or visual reference board
- **Why it matters:** Either the client genuinely needs execution (in which case charge for execution, not concept), or they're about to have their thinking challenged and be upset about it
- **How to surface:** Clarify the mandate: "Is the direction locked and we're executing, or is it a starting point we can push back on?"

## Brief-as-document red flags

### The brief is a pitch deck
- **Looks like:** 40 slides, lots of inspiration imagery, no real brief content
- **Why it matters:** The client's marketing team has confused "communicating internally" with "briefing externally"
- **How to surface:** Extract the brief-shaped content and flag that the rest is context, not brief

### The brief is an RFP
- **Looks like:** "Please respond with your approach, team, case studies, and pricing by {date}"
- **Why it matters:** This is a pitch document, not a brief. The sharpener is the wrong tool.
- **How to surface:** Redirect to `/rfi-response` or `/proposal-generator`

### The brief is an email thread
- **Looks like:** Forwarded emails, "here's what we've discussed so far"
- **Why it matters:** Not all content in the thread is brief content — some is sales, some is complaints, some is context
- **How to surface:** Extract the brief-shaped signal, flag the noise, ask the client to confirm priorities

### The brief is 1-2 sentences
- **Looks like:** "We need a new website. Budget ~£50k. Let us know if you can help."
- **Why it matters:** Not a brief. Not enough to score confidently.
- **How to surface:** Don't score. Route to `/creative-brief` From scratch mode or suggest a 30-minute discovery call.

## How to use this file

1. **Read every time** — not just when something seems off. The flags you don't notice are the ones that cause problems.
2. **Flag every match, not just project-killers** — the client still decides; your job is to surface.
3. **Never accuse the client** — the flags are about the *brief*, not the people. "This brief has a budget-scope mismatch" not "The client doesn't understand what things cost."
4. **Cite the pattern** — if you name a flag in the output, reference the pattern by name so the user can read up.
5. **Match flags to questions** — every flag usually maps to a Tier 1 or Tier 2 question in the output.

## Adding patterns

When a skill run surfaces a red flag that isn't in this library, the
compounding loop at the end of the skill will offer to save it to
`learnings.md § Client Patterns` so future sharpener runs can
