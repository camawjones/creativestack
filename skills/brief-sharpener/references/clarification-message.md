# Client Clarification Message

The brief-sharpener's most actionable output for creatives is a
*drafted message you can actually send to the client*. Not just a
list of questions — a warm, professional, ready-to-send message
that reflects the work you've already put in and asks the questions
that matter.

This is the difference between a skill that makes creatives feel
informed and a skill that moves the project forward.

---

## When to draft a clarification message

**Always draft one unless:**
- Mode is **Internal brief** (no client to send to)
- Mode is **Triage** (the user is deciding whether to engage at all)
- The brief scores 8+ with no Red dimensions (there's nothing material to ask)
- The user explicitly opts out

Otherwise, draft one. Creatives should be able to copy-paste it into
email or Slack with minor tweaks.

---

## Tone calibration

Before drafting, check:

1. **`tone-of-voice.md`** in the brain — use the user's own voice
   if it exists
2. **`clients.md`** for this client — if there's a record of
   previous communication tone with this specific client, match it
3. **Nothing in brain** — default to the "warm, direct, light
   touch" voice described below

### Default voice

- **Warm, not formal.** "Hey {name}" or "Hi {name}" — never "Dear {name}"
- **Direct, not hedgy.** Don't apologise for asking questions. Don't say "just wanted to check" or "sorry to bother".
- **Confident, not cocky.** You've read the brief carefully — the questions should sound like the work you've done, not a checklist you filled in.
- **Light, not stiff.** Short sentences. Contractions. One-line paragraphs are fine.
- **First-person plural.** "We've been through the brief..." — it's the team's perspective, not the individual creative's.

### Never
- Don't list grievances ("the budget is unclear, the timeline is impossible...")
- Don't sound defensive ("before we can start we'll need...")
- Don't use "I'm reaching out to..." or "I hope this finds you well"
- Don't ask more than 5 questions in the message (save Tier 3s for the kickoff call)
- Don't send a questionnaire — it's a message, not a form

---

## Structure

Every clarification message follows this shape:

```
{Warm opener — 1 sentence}

{Appreciation + signal you've read it — 1-2 sentences}

{Context framing — 1 sentence — why you're asking}

{The questions — top 3-5, Tier 1 first, numbered}

{Offer — make it easy to respond — 1 sentence}

{Warm close — 1 line}
```

Total length: 120-200 words. Anything longer feels like homework.

---

## The opener

One sentence. Warm but not overly familiar.

**Good openers:**
- "Hey {name} — thanks for sending this across."
- "Morning {name} — just finished reading through the brief."
- "Hi {name} — really looking forward to digging into this one."

**Bad openers:**
- "Dear {name}, I hope this email finds you well." (too formal)
- "Quick one..." (undersells the work)
- "So..." (unprofessional)

---

## The appreciation + signal

Show that you've read the brief carefully. Reference something
specific from it. This earns the right to ask questions.

**Examples:**
- "The {specific project detail} context is really helpful — gives us a clear starting point."
- "Really liked the framing around {specific theme} — it's the kind of challenge the team will enjoy."
- "The {specific section} gave us a good sense of where the business is at."

Specificity is key. Generic appreciation ("thanks for the detailed brief") signals the opposite of attention.

---

## The context framing

One sentence that explains *why* you're asking questions without making
it sound like the brief is deficient.

**Good framings:**
- "Before we get the team sketching, there are a few things worth locking in so we don't waste anyone's time going in the wrong direction."
- "A couple of things jumped out that are worth a quick conversation before the creative kicks off."
- "We've spotted a few things that would shape the work significantly depending on which way they go."

**Bad framings:**
- "The brief is missing some things..." (makes the client feel bad)
- "We need clarification on several points..." (bureaucratic)
- "In order to proceed we require..." (stiff)

---

## The questions (Tier 1 first, then 1-2 Tier 2)

Maximum 5 questions in the email. The rest stay in the internal
output for the kickoff call.

Phrase per `question-laddering.md` rules:
- Easy to answer
- Options where possible
- Framed around client benefit
- One thing per question
- No jargon

**Format the questions as a numbered list** — visually scannable,
easy to answer inline.

**Don't label them Tier 1 / Tier 2 in the email** — that's
internal language. Just list them in order of importance.

### Special handling

**For budget questions:**
Always offer a range, not a blank. "Is this more in the £20k, £50k,
£100k, or £250k+ range?" Clients answer ranges even when they'd
refuse a direct figure.

**For real-ask questions:**
Use the 5-whys ladder question from `question-laddering.md`.
"If this work does everything you hope, what changes in the
business in 6 months that wouldn't happen otherwise?" is better
than "what's the real objective?".

**For contradiction questions:**
Cite the specific contradiction from the brief. "The brief mentions
'premium positioning' in the objectives and 'price-sensitive
audience' in the audience section — which takes priority if they
conflict?"

**For stakeholder questions:**
Frame around protecting the client's time: "So we can make sure
{key stakeholder} sees the work at the right stage, who's the
final creative sign-off?"

---

## The offer

Make responding easy. One sentence that lowers the effort.

**Examples:**
- "Happy to jump on a 20-minute call if that's faster than typing it all out."
- "A few quick notes back is fine — doesn't need to be formal."
- "If it's easier, we could cover this in a 15-minute call tomorrow."

The offer signals that you care about their time and that the
questions are a means to an end, not bureaucracy.

---

## The close

Warm, brief, forward-looking.

**Good closes:**
- "Excited to get stuck in."
- "Looking forward to hearing your thoughts."
- "Thanks — and excited for this one."

Never:
- "Please advise." (bureaucratic)
- "Let me know ASAP." (aggressive)
- "Best regards," alone (too formal for warm voice)

---

## Format variants

The skill should draft the message in *one* primary format chosen
to match the brain's tone-of-voice default or the client's preferred
channel if known from `clients.md`.

### Email (default)
Full opener, full structure, sign-off. Good for new clients or
clients who communicate primarily by email.

### Slack / Teams
Tighter. No "Hi {name}" — Slack threads don't need it. Can use
bold for the question lead-ins. Shorter close ("ta!" or "cheers!"
depending on tone).

### WhatsApp / iMessage
Rare but real for some creative relationships. Very short. Pick
the 1-2 highest-priority questions only. Save the rest for email.

### In-person / call notes
If the user says "we have a kickoff call next week", reframe the
output as talking points for the call rather than a written
message. Same questions, same tiering, but delivered as "things to
cover in the call".

## Example output (email format, agency voice)

```
Hey Marcus —

Thanks for sending the brief over. The context around the retail
expansion is really helpful, and the "we want to feel less corporate"
note gives us something concrete to work with.

Before we get the team sketching, a few things jumped out that
would shape the direction meaningfully:

1. When you imagine this landing in 6 months, what's changed for
   the business that wouldn't have changed otherwise? That helps
   us make sure we're solving the right problem, not just the
   stated one.

2. The brief mentions "premium but approachable" — if we had to
   pick one as the lead in the work, which wins? We can usually
   hold both, but only with a clear priority.

3. Ballpark budget — are we planning against something more in the
   £50k, £100k, or £250k+ range? Even a broad range helps us match
   ambition to scope from the start.

4. Who's the final creative sign-off internally? Asking so we make
   sure they're in the room at the right stage, not at the end.

Happy to jump on 20 minutes tomorrow if that's quicker than typing
it all out.

Excited for this one.

{sign-off}
```

Total: ~190 words. 4 questions. One Tier 1 (real ask), one Tier 2
(tone contradiction), one Tier 1 (budget), one Tier 1 (decision-maker).

---

## What's NOT in the message

Creatives should be crystal clear on what isn't going in the email
but is still in the internal output:

- The full rubric (internal only)
- Red flag pattern names (internal only)
- Fee/team/timeline sanity check details (internal only)
- Tier 3 refinement questions (save for kickoff)
- Competitive/trend blind spots that don't materially change the
  questions (internal only — raise in kickoff if relevant)
- Any critique of the brief itself (never — criticise upward)

The output format section in SKILL.md should make this split clear:
**"For internal use"** sections and **"For the client"** sections
should be visually separated so the user knows what to copy.

---

## Variants by mode

- **Triage mode:** No clarification message. Triage is about
  deciding whether to engage; questions come later.
- **Sharpen mode (default):** Full clarification message per above.
- **Re-brief diff mode:** Message focuses on *what changed* and the
  questions that the changes raised. Don't re-ask old questions.
- **Internal brief mode:** No client message — instead, draft a
  set of "questions for the internal stakeholder" in a similar
  shape but with internal-language framing.
