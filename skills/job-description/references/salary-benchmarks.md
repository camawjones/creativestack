# Salary Benchmarks

Indicative ranges for creative roles in the UK and US. **These are starting points,
not gospel.** Salary norms shift annually and vary significantly by city, agency
size, and specialism. Always sense-check against current market data — Major Players,
Creative Equals, Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary Insights, and the BD Salary Survey
(US/global) are good cross-references.

**Last reviewed:** 2026-04. Re-check at least annually.

Outside London (Manchester, Bristol, Edinburgh, Leeds), the lower end of the range is
the norm. Fully remote: anchor to the lower-mid of the range unless the candidate
brings London-rate experience.

## How to use these ranges

1. **Match the role to the closest template, then to the level.** Don't blend levels —
   if the responsibilities are senior, the salary needs to be senior, full stop.
2. **Compare the user's offered salary to the band:**
   - **Below the bottom of range:** flag as a salary/seniority mismatch. Either the
     salary is too low or the role is being mis-leveled.
   - **Within range:** confirm and note where in the band (lower / mid / upper).
   - **Above the band:** flag positively — this is a competitive differentiator and
     should be foregrounded in the JD.
3. **Watch the city/remote adjustment.** A "Senior Designer, fully remote" listing at
   £75k will pull London applicants. The same listing at £45k will not.
4. **Specialist roles command premiums.** Motion designers with strong 3D, devs with
   creative coding chops, strategists with category specialism (luxury, B2B SaaS,
   regulated industries) — add 10–20% to the standard range.
5. **Agency size matters.** Boutiques pay below the band but offer ownership and
   broader work. Network agencies pay top of band but with structure overhead. Be
   honest about what you can offer — pay or experience.

## Output flag templates

If salary is below band:
> **Salary flag:** £{amount} is below typical UK market rate for a {level} {role}
> (£{range}). This will reduce applicant quality, deter senior candidates, and
> signal a mismatch between the responsibilities described and what's on the table.
> Consider raising the offer, re-leveling the role, or being explicit about
> non-cash compensation (equity, profit share, time, learning budget).

If salary is significantly above band:
> **Salary signal:** £{amount} sits comfortably above the {level} {role} band
> (£{range}). This is a real differentiator — consider foregrounding it in the
> "What We Offer" section rather than burying it.

If no salary provided:
> **Transparency note:** No salary band was given. Posting without a salary band
> reduces applications by ~30% and disproportionately deters women and
> underrepresented candidates (multiple studies). Recommend including a band
> (e.g., "£{lower}–£{upper}, depending on experience"). It also avoids wasting
> everyone's time at offer stage.
