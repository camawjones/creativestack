# Copy Atoms

Copy isn't a monolith. A good copy deck treats each **atom** — the smallest
unit of copy that serves a distinct purpose — on its own terms. A headline
has different success criteria than a body paragraph. A button label has
nothing in common with a legal footer.

This file defines the atoms, what each is for, and how to judge each.

The skill evaluates and scores copy at the atom level, not the section
level. A section can be "mostly on voice" while specific atoms inside it
fail — atom-level scoring catches what section-level scoring misses.

---

## The atom taxonomy

### 1. Headline

**Purpose:** Stop the reader. Earn the next 3 seconds of their attention.

**Quality criteria:**
- Specific, not generic
- Uses a concrete noun where possible
- Avoids tense-less phrases ("Introducing" or "The future of")
- Promises something the body can deliver on
- Works in isolation (scannable without context)

**What "good" looks like:**
> "Run faster. Recover harder. Without the shoes you have to coach."
> (Specific, concrete, promises something)

**What bad looks like:**
> "Introducing our new running collection for modern athletes."
> (Template headline — could belong to any brand)

**Length guidance:**
- Web hero: 4-10 words
- Email subject: 5-8 words
- Social headline: 5-12 words
- Print: flexible, but shorter usually wins

### 2. Subhead / Deck

**Purpose:** Expand the headline's promise by one layer. Give the reader
a reason to keep reading into the body.

**Quality criteria:**
- Adds information, doesn't restate the headline
- Completes the promise the headline opened
- Written to be read in one breath
- Never a full paragraph

**What good looks like:**
> **Headline:** "Run faster. Recover harder."
> **Subhead:** "The first shoe tested by ultra marathoners instead of focus groups."

**What bad looks like:**
> **Headline:** "Run faster. Recover harder."
> **Subhead:** "Our new collection helps runners perform better and feel great."
> (Restates the headline in softer language)

**Length guidance:** 1 sentence, 10-20 words.

### 3. Body

**Purpose:** Deliver on the promise. Explain. Persuade. Move the reader
toward the desired response.

**Quality criteria:**
- Earns every sentence (no filler)
- Short sentences outperform long ones in most contexts
- Specific proof over abstract claims
- Rhythmic variation (short, short, longer, short)
- No sentence that could apply to any brand
- Paragraphs rarely longer than 3 sentences

**Body hierarchy (in long-form):**
1. **Opening** — hooks, expands the promise
2. **Middle** — proof, specifics, stories, evidence
3. **Close** — pivots toward the CTA

**What good looks like:**
> "We didn't test this shoe in a lab. We tested it on 100 ultra runners
> across six continents. 94 of them broke their previous times in the
> first race. The six who didn't told us what to change. Then we shipped it."
> (Specific, concrete, short sentences, proof-driven)

**What bad looks like:**
> "Our cutting-edge running shoes are designed with the latest technology
> to provide unparalleled performance and comfort for athletes of all
> levels. Discover how our innovative approach to running footwear can
> transform your running experience."
> (Generic, long sentences, no proof, could be any brand)

### 4. CTA (call to action)

**Purpose:** Tell the reader exactly what to do next. Reduce friction.

**Quality criteria:**
- Action verb first
- Specific about the action ("Get the shoe" beats "Learn more")
- Matches the commitment level of the reader's current state
- Never starts with "Click" or "Tap" (infrastructure leakage)

**What good looks like:**
- "Get your pair"
- "See the proof"
- "Book a fitting"
- "Start the trial"

**What bad looks like:**
- "Click here"
- "Learn more"
- "Find out how"
- "Submit"

**Length guidance:** 2-5 words. Longer only if the CTA is a full-sentence
invitation ("Try it for 30 days, risk-free.").

**CTA hierarchy on a page:**
- **Primary CTA:** the action we most want (one per page)
- **Secondary CTA:** an alternative for users not ready to commit
- **Tertiary CTA:** low-commitment next steps (read more, sign up for updates)

### 5. Microcopy — Buttons

**Purpose:** Label an action in UI.

**Quality criteria:**
- Verb-first ("Add to cart", not "Cart")
- Consistent tense and voice across the product
- Matches the brand's tone even in small moments
- Never "Submit" (generic, scary)

See `microcopy-patterns.md` for the full button vocabulary.

### 6. Microcopy — Errors

**Purpose:** Tell the user what went wrong, what to do about it, and
(ideally) reassure them.

**Quality criteria:**
- Plain language, no jargon
- Identifies the problem specifically
- Tells the user what to do next
- Tone matches the brand even under stress
- Never blames the user

**What good looks like:**
> "Couldn't find that email. Check for typos, or try a different one."

**What bad looks like:**
> "Invalid input. Please try again."

See `microcopy-patterns.md` for error patterns.

### 7. Microcopy — Empty states

**Purpose:** Turn an empty screen into an opportunity. Explain why it's
empty, what to do, and (optionally) add personality.

**Quality criteria:**
- Never just "No items"
- Explains why and what to do
- Adds a touch of brand personality
- Gives a clear action

**What good looks like:**
> "Nothing here yet. Add your first project and we'll get you running."

See `microcopy-patterns.md` for empty state patterns.

### 8. Microcopy — Loading / Success / Confirmation

**Purpose:** Cover the moments between user action and system response.
These are the most overlooked atoms and the ones where voice is most visible.

See `microcopy-patterns.md`.

### 9. Microcopy — Form helpers / Placeholders / Labels

**Purpose:** Guide the user to fill a form correctly the first time.

**Quality criteria:**
- Labels are clear, never cute
- Placeholders show format, not instructions
- Helper text goes below the field, explains why or how
- Error states tie back to helper text

See `microcopy-patterns.md`.

### 10. Captions / Alt text / Image descriptions

**Purpose:** Serve the image without distracting from it. For alt text,
serve accessibility.

**Quality criteria:**
- Captions add context the image alone doesn't show
- Alt text describes what's in the image, briefly, functionally
- Neither is a place for wit at the expense of clarity
- Alt text is accessibility, not marketing

**Caption vs alt text:**
- **Caption:** "Ana Liu, ultra runner, testing the Air Zoom in Patagonia."
- **Alt text:** "A runner in a blue jacket on a mountain trail."

### 11. Legal / Disclaimer / Compliance

**Purpose:** Fulfil legal or regulatory obligations. Protect the brand.

**Quality criteria:**
- Accurate (legal signs off, not the copywriter)
- Readable if possible (plain legal is better than dense legal)
- Positioned where required, never hidden deceptively
- Never squeezed into brand voice so tightly that it misrepresents

**Never:**
- Make the disclaimer "fun" at the expense of clarity
- Use brand voice conventions that could obscure the meaning
- Paraphrase regulated language

### 12. SEO meta — Title / Description / OG

**Purpose:** Earn the click from search or social preview.

**Quality criteria:**
- Meta title: 50-60 characters, primary keyword toward the start
- Meta description: 150-160 characters, promises what the page delivers
- Open Graph title and description are often different from meta (more conversational)
- Never generated as an afterthought

**What good looks like:**
> **Meta title:** "Ultra Running Shoes | Tested by Ultra Runners | Nike Air"
> **Meta description:** "The first shoe tested in real races by real ultra runners. 94% of testers broke their personal bests. See the proof."

**What bad looks like:**
> **Meta title:** "Home | Nike"
> **Meta description:** "Welcome to Nike, where we sell shoes and athletic apparel for runners and athletes of all levels."

### 13. Voice of the brand — Manifesto / About / Positioning

**Purpose:** State who the brand is and why it exists. The most brand-exposed
form of copy — every word carries voice weight.

**Quality criteria:**
- The single-minded thought from the brief must show up here explicitly
- Written in the brand's voice, not the agency's
- Short > long (manifestos rarely benefit from length)
- Specific > universal (universal manifestos sound like everyone)
- Never generic aspirational language ("We believe", "We imagine")

See `voice-sharpness-claims.md` for the voice scoring methodology.

## Atom-level scoring

Every atom in a copy deck gets scored on 4 dimensions:

| Dimension | What it measures |
|---|---|
| **Voice match** | Does it match the brand's voice per `projects/{slug}-brief.md § Brand Tone & Personality`? |
| **Sharpness** | Is it specific and earned, or flat and generic? |
| **SMT ladder** | Does it connect to the brief's Single-Minded Thought? |
| **Craft** | Is the writing itself tight — no filler, active voice where appropriate, right length? |

See `voice-sharpness-claims.md` for the scoring methodology.

Atoms score 1-5 per dimension. The deck's overall score averages across all
atoms. Red flags are atoms scoring 1-2 on any dimension — these get
highlighted in the output regardless of overall score.

## Choosing which atoms to include

Not every deck needs every atom. Use the project type from the brief to
determine which atoms are in scope:

| Project type | Primary atoms | Secondary | Often skipped |
|---|---|---|---|
| Campaign | Headline, subhead, body, CTA, social variants | Legal, SEO meta | Microcopy, form helpers |
| Website | Headline, subhead, body, CTA, microcopy, SEO meta | Empty states, legal | Manifesto (unless about page) |
| Digital product | Microcopy (all types), CTAs, body, helper text | Headlines, SEO meta | Manifesto, long-form body |
| Brand / identity | Manifesto, positioning, tone rules, sample headlines | Body samples, CTAs | Microcopy, SEO meta |
| Email | Subject line, preheader, headline, body, CTA | Alt text for images | Microcopy, SEO meta |
| Social | Platform-specific headline/body, CTAs, captions | Alt text | SEO meta, legal |
| Print / OOH | Headline, subhead, body, CTA, legal | Captions | Microcopy, SEO meta |
| Presentation | Slide headlines, body bullets, speaker notes | Section dividers | SEO meta, microcopy |
| Packaging | Front-of-pack hero, benefit lines, back-of-pack body, legal | Alt text | SEO meta, manifesto |

If the user hasn't specified atoms, start from the project type's primary
list and ask once about secondary atoms: "Do you also need {secondary
atoms} for this deck?"

## Missing atom detection

When generating the deck, flag atoms that are expected for the project type
but missing from the input:

> **Missing atoms flagged:**
> - **Email subject lines** — the deck covers body copy but not subjects.
>   Subject lines do 80% of the work for email open rates.
> - **Error microcopy** — the deck covers form labels and helpers but not
>   error states. These are usually written at QA time and suffer for it.

Don't silently generate placeholder atoms for missing inputs. Flag and ask.

## Atom naming convention

In the output, atoms should be labelled consistently so the client and
the dev team can reference them cleanly:

```
H1 — Hero headline
H2 — Hero subhead
BODY — Hero body
CTA — Hero primary CTA
CTA2 — Hero secondary CTA
SEO_TITLE — Meta title
SEO_DESC — Meta description
BTN_SAVE — Save button
ERR_INVALID_EMAIL — Invalid email error
EMPTY_PROJECTS — Empty projects state
```

This convention makes it easy for developers to map copy to code and for
clients to reference specific atoms in feedback.
