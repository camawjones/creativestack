## What this file is

**Source:** Cameron Jones practitioner view — not survey data.
These benchmarks reflect what I see in creative businesses I work
with and talk to. They are directional, not statistical.

**Why they exist:** An absolute score of "3/5 on Ops" is useless
without context. Creative leaders running this audit for their CEO
need to answer "3/5 — is that good?" in one sentence. These
benchmarks give them that sentence.

**What they are not:**
- Not survey data
- Not industry research
- Not a substitute for a proper benchmarking engagement
- Not a leaderboard

Every output that cites benchmarks must label them as practitioner
view, not research. Credibility dies the moment a CEO asks "where's
the source" and there isn't one.

## How to use it

1. **Match the user's discipline and size** to a row in the matrix
2. **Report the baseline** alongside their current score
3. **Frame the gap** — "typical for your segment" / "ahead of
   typical" / "behind typical"
4. **Never claim precision** — "roughly 2.5-3 for studios your
   size" not "the industry average is 2.7"

### Size brackets

| Bracket | Range |
|---|---|
| **Solo** | 1 person |
| **Micro** | 2-5 people |
| **Small** | 6-15 people |
| **Mid** | 16-40 people |
| **Large** | 41-100 people |
| **Major** | 100+ people |

Use bracket, not exact size. A 12-person studio and a 15-person
studio are the same for benchmarking purposes.

## Per-category notes

Observations about *which category moves fastest* in each segment
— useful for prioritising quick wins.

### Ops & Admin
- Fastest-moving category across all segments
- Biggest win for Solo and Micro
- Plateaus at 3-4 without dedicated ops hire
- Large/Major hit 4 consistently; 5 requires deliberate investment

### Creative Workflow
- Slowest-moving category; creative cultures resist process
- 3 is the ceiling without explicit workflow hire
- Scoring 4+ usually means disciplined PM layer
- Scoring 5 is almost never real — flag for suspicion

### Client/Stakeholder Communication
- Middle-speed category
- Usually improves by accident as agencies grow
- 4+ requires explicit templating and cadence
- In-house teams typically lag here due to volume of stakeholders

### Knowledge & IP
- Most under-invested category across all segments
- Scoring 2 is the norm, even at Mid and Large
- Moving to 3 is the single highest-leverage move available
- Solo/Micro almost never score above 2 without explicit effort

### Commercials & Intelligence
- Second-slowest category
- Small and below rarely get past 2
- Mid is where visibility starts to emerge
- Large/Major often at 3-4 but data isn't acted on

## Protected zone baselines

Typical Protected Zone status by segment:

| Segment | Typical | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Solo freelancer | Weak-Partial | "Protected zone" usually not articulated; creative instinct implicit |
| Micro studio | Partial | Founder-led creative culture, defended by personality not process |
| Small agency | Partial-Strong | First PM hires force articulation |
| Mid agency | Partial | Politics suppresses explicit defence |
| Large agency | Weak-Partial | Scale erodes the defended perimeter |
| In-house | Weak | Stakeholder pressure erodes protection fastest |

**Insight for output:** smaller = stronger protected zone by
default (implicit). Larger = weaker protected zone (erosion).
Running this audit with protected zone scoring is especially
valuable for Mid/Large segments where the erosion is invisible.

## Caveats

**These benchmarks are:**
- Practitioner-informed estimates, not research
- A starting point for conversation, not a verdict
- Updated as the market evolves (this file should refresh at least
  yearly; check `last_updated` frontmatter when available)
- Biased toward the segments Cameron Jones works with most
  (design-led, brand-adjacent, UK/EU)

**These benchmarks are not:**
- A claim about "industry standard"
- A replacement for formal benchmarking
- Stable — AI maturity is a fast-moving target; 2026 numbers will
  look naive by 2027

**How to cite in outputs:**

> "Typical for your segment (Cameron Jones practitioner view, 2026)."

Never:

> "The industry average is..."
> "Research shows..."
> "Studies indicate..."

The honesty is the differentiator.
