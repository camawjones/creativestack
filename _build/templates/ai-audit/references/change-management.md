## The three fears every creative team has about AI

These are near-universal. Every change management output should
acknowledge them before proposing anything.

### 1. "My craft will be devalued"
The fear that the thing the creative is good at — the thing that
took years to develop — is about to be commodified. Often expressed
as "I didn't train for five years to be a prompt engineer."

**Why it's real:** Sometimes the craft *is* being devalued in
specific narrow ways (stock illustration, commodity copy). The
fear is not irrational.

**Why it's overblown:** The craft that matters — judgment,
composition, taste, editorial eye — is *harder*, not easier, to
automate. The fear points at a real thing but usually in the wrong
direction.

**How to address it:** Name specific things the team is good at
and confirm those stay protected. "Your concept reviews, your
composition calls, your taste on typography — these are the things
that separate your work from wallpaper. We're not touching them."

---

### 2. "I'll be replaced"
The fear that AI will make the creative's job economically
unnecessary.

**Why it's real:** Some jobs at some levels will compress. Junior
roles that were mostly production work are changing.

**Why it's overblown:** Creative output without human judgment is
wallpaper. The market is learning this fast. Agencies that automate
the judgment go out of business; agencies that automate the drag
around the judgment compound.

**How to address it:** Be specific about what AI is *doing* in
your plan — admin, drafting, research, first passes. Be equally
specific about what it's *not* doing — the final call, the client
conversation, the concept review. If you can't be specific, don't
introduce the tool.

---

### 3. "The work will get worse"
The fear that AI-generated output will degrade the quality bar the
team has worked to establish, and they'll be asked to put their
names on work they don't believe in.

**Why it's real:** This happens constantly with badly-implemented
AI. It's the single biggest reputational risk.

**Why it's overblown:** Only when AI is used for final output. AI
as a drafting, research, and admin tool — with a human reviewer
before the work ships — consistently *raises* the bar because it
frees up time for the judgment layer.

**How to address it:** Make the quality gate explicit. "Every
piece of AI-assisted work passes through {named person} before it
ships. Nobody's name goes on slop."

## Anxieties by discipline

Tailor the framing to the specific discipline of the user.

### Brand / identity
- **Primary anxiety:** "Design decisions become template choices"
- **Secondary:** "The craft of typography and composition gets commodified"
- **Framing:** "AI captures the *description* of the design decision. The decision stays with the designer. We're not using AI for concept or composition — we're using it for research, moodboards, and the admin around the work."

### Digital product
- **Primary anxiety:** "Our process becomes indistinguishable from everyone else's"
- **Secondary:** "Design system work becomes filling in AI-generated templates"
- **Framing:** "AI accelerates the boring parts — spec writing, component documentation, handoff notes — so we have more time for the hard parts: interaction design, information architecture, the calls that actually affect how the product feels."

### Motion / animation
- **Primary anxiety:** "AI video generation replaces hand craft"
- **Secondary:** "Our asset pipeline becomes a commodity service"
- **Framing:** "We're not touching the craft work. AI is handling brief intake, reference gathering, asset management, scheduling — everything around the motion work, not the motion work itself."

### Copy / content
- **Primary anxiety:** "My voice becomes an LLM output"
- **Secondary:** "Editing AI drafts feels like losing the creative act"
- **Framing:** "We're *encoding* your voice, not replacing it. The tone-of-voice work makes AI-drafted material sound more like you, not less. You still own every final word, and the decision of what to write about stays with you."

### Illustration
- **Primary anxiety:** "AI illustration tools make me obsolete"
- **Secondary:** "Clients will expect me to compete with generated imagery on price"
- **Framing:** "AI handles reference gathering, mood boards, and admin. The drawing, the style, the craft — those stay with you. Clients who want generated imagery aren't your clients; clients who want *your work* are more loyal when they see what AI can't do."

### Strategy / research
- **Primary anxiety:** "Research becomes table stakes instead of a service"
- **Secondary:** "Insight work gets confused with data summarisation"
- **Framing:** "AI does desk research and summarisation. *Insight* — the non-obvious truth that changes what a team would make — is the thing AI can't do, and making it more obvious what only you can do raises your value, not lowers it."

### Multi-disciplinary
- **Primary anxiety:** "Coordination work was the glue and now it's automated away"
- **Secondary:** "Each discipline gets hit with its own version of the fears above"
- **Framing:** "Coordination automation makes the creative hand-offs cleaner, not the creative work cheaper. And we're addressing each discipline's specific concerns separately — not a one-size change."

## Anxieties by team size

### Solo
- **Primary anxiety:** "I'm competing with AI-native freelancers"
- **Framing:** "You're not. You're competing on judgment and relationship, both of which AI can't replicate. AI closes the admin gap so you can spend more time on the work that wins the work."

### Micro (2-5)
- **Primary anxiety:** "We can't afford to experiment with this properly"
- **Framing:** "You can afford not to. The whole point of this stack is that the experimentation cost is small. Pick one category — ops, or knowledge — and move it this month."

### Small (6-15)
- **Primary anxiety:** "We've got just enough process to be dangerous; AI will break it"
- **Framing:** "AI fits *into* the process you have. The quick wins are shaped to strengthen your process, not replace it."

### Mid (16-40)
- **Primary anxiety:** "Politics will block this before it ships"
- **Framing:** "This is exactly why the 30-60-90 plan is sequenced around consent. The first phase is additive — nothing gets taken away from anyone. Earn consent in phase 1, expand in phase 2 and 3."

### Large (41+)
- **Primary anxiety:** "We've tried this before and it became a procurement exercise"
- **Framing:** "This isn't a tool rollout. It's a set of workflow changes supported by specific tools. The tools are the least important part. Leading with workflow avoids the procurement trap."

## Framings to use verbatim

These are opening lines the user can paste directly into a team
message, email, or all-hands slide. They land because they open
with what's *protected*, not what's changing.

### For any team announcement

> "Here's what we're doing with AI and here's what we're not. We're
> using AI to remove friction around the work — admin, research,
> first drafts, scheduling. We're not using it for concepts, craft
> decisions, or client conversations. That stays with us, by
> design. We'll review how it's going at 30, 60, and 90 days and
> adjust based on what we learn."

### For a CEO / leadership pitch

> "Most agencies are approaching AI as a cost-cutting exercise.
> We're approaching it as a craft-protection exercise. The
> automation layer frees up time for the judgment layer — where
> our value actually lives. This plan sequences quick wins that
> pay back within the first month against investments that
> compound over the year."

### For a team member who's nervous

> "Tell me the part of your job you'd be devastated to lose. That's
> the part we're protecting. Everything else is negotiable, and I
> want your input on what stays, what changes, and what we run
> next."

### For a team member who's overly enthusiastic

> "Good energy. Let's channel it into one quick win this week so
> we have evidence before we make it a strategy. Pick the thing
> that's been annoying you longest and let's see if we can fix it."

## The protected-zone statement

A one-paragraph statement the user can adopt, sign, or publish
internally. Adjust the specifics per discipline.

```
{Team / agency / practice name} uses AI to remove friction from
our operational work: admin, research, drafting, scheduling, and
knowledge capture. We do not use AI for:

- {Discipline-specific craft decision — e.g. "typography and
  composition judgments", or "concept development and interaction
  design", or "tone and voice calibration for client work"}
- Client-facing creative conversations, pitches, and presentations
- Final creative output that goes out under our name
- Decisions about pricing, scope, or people

Every piece of AI-assisted work passes through a named reviewer
before it ships. We review this policy quarterly as the tools and
the work change.
```

Offer this as a copy-paste block in the output, clearly marked as
"Adapt and adopt — this is a starting point, not a finished policy."

## 30-60-90 day change plan template

### Days 1-30: Additive only
- **Rule:** Nothing gets taken away. All changes are additions.
- **Wins:** Pick 1-2 quick wins from the lowest-scoring category.
  Run them on real work. Keep the rest of the process intact.
- **Success criteria:** At least one team member says "this made
  my week easier." Nothing breaks. Nobody complains that quality
  dropped.
- **Review gate:** 30-day team check-in. Three questions: What
  helped? What was noise? What surprised you?

### Days 31-60: Integrate the winners
- **Rule:** Lock in what worked in days 1-30. Begin replacing
  old process only for things that are demonstrably better.
- **Wins:** Add 2-3 more from the next category. Start building
  the brain if you haven't.
- **Success criteria:** The wins from phase 1 are now business-as-
  usual. No one is reverting to old workflows because the new
  ones are worse.
- **Review gate:** 60-day team check-in. Same three questions, plus:
  "What should we try next?"

### Days 61-90: Expand and measure
- **Rule:** The change is real now. Apply to more of the practice.
- **Wins:** Tackle the hardest category (usually Knowledge & IP
  or Creative Workflow). Consider process redesigns, not just
  tool additions.
- **Success criteria:** Re-run this audit and compare delta against
  the baseline. Aim for +0.5 to +1.0 on the worked categories.
- **Review gate:** 90-day team check-in + audit delta. If the
  delta is positive and the team is calm, scale. If either isn't,
  pause and diagnose.

### What this plan deliberately does not include
- A tool selection process (tools are the least important part)
- KPIs that incentivise AI use for its own sake
- Mandatory adoption targets
- Cost-cutting targets
- "AI transformation" language

## Handling the skeptic

Every team has one. They're usually right about more than people
give them credit for. The skeptic is not the problem — they're the
quality gate.

### The skeptic's argument (usually)
"This is all hype. Last time we rolled out a 'transformation' it
cost us months and the tools never got used. The work is fine. If
it ain't broke, don't fix it."

### What's right about it
- Previous transformation attempts probably *were* badly run
- The work *is* often fine; the problem is operational drag, not
  creative quality
- Tool rollouts that don't change workflows never stick
- Hype is a real problem in the AI space right now

### What to do about it

1. **Acknowledge the pattern.** "You're right that tool rollouts
   usually fail. That's why we're starting with the workflow, not
   the tool."
2. **Give them the review gate role.** Not a veto, but a formal
   seat at the 30/60/90 checkpoints. Empowered skeptics become
   quality owners.
3. **Pick wins they care about.** If the skeptic's biggest gripe
   is "the briefs are always terrible", start with `/brief-sharpener`.
   Addressing their specific pain builds consent faster than arguing.
4. **Let them kill any win that isn't working.** If phase 1 has
   a win the skeptic flags as noise, cut it. Credibility earned
   in phase 1 pays back in phase 2 and 3.

### What not to do

- Don't ignore them
- Don't argue that AI is the future
- Don't assign the skeptic the AI implementation role (this is the
  single most common mistake and it almost always fails)
- Don't separate them from the decision loop

## Anti-patterns (what not to do)

### Leading with ROI numbers
Creatives distrust spreadsheets about creative work. Leading with
"£14k in saved time per year" starts the conversation in the wrong
frame. Lead with what's protected; put ROI in the second slide.

### "Everyone else is doing it"
This is the worst possible framing for a creative team. They got
into creative work to *not* do what everyone else is doing. Never
use "our competitors are" as a justification.

### Secret pilots
Running AI tools in the background without telling the team erodes
trust when discovered. And it will be discovered. Always declare
what's being used, by whom, for what.

### Mandating use
Mandatory adoption produces malicious compliance — people technically
use the tool, technically hate the output, and the whole experiment
dies in cynicism. Adoption through demonstration, not mandate.

### Skipping the protected-zone statement
Teams that don't have an explicit protected zone assume the worst.
An explicit statement is the cheapest possible insurance.

### Running this audit once and filing it
The audit only compounds with re-runs. Filing the first audit in a
drawer is the single biggest failure mode. The 90-day review gate
is not optional; it's the thing that makes the change real.
