# Role Templates

Baselines for common creative roles. Use as a starting point — adapt to the user's
specific brief. Don't output the template raw. The point is to make sure nothing
obvious is missed and that level distinctions stay honest.

For each role: typical responsibilities by level, core skills, common tools, typical
reporting line, and what genuinely separates one level from the next.

---

## Designer (visual / brand / digital)

**Core skills:** typography, layout, hierarchy, brand systems, art direction sensibility, file craft
**Common tools:** Figma, Adobe CC (Illustrator, Photoshop, InDesign), Sketch (legacy)
**Typical reporting line:** Design Lead → Creative Director

### Junior (0–2 years)
- Executes within a defined system; doesn't build the system
- Strong file hygiene, follows brand guidelines accurately
- Needs direction on hierarchy and concept; growing in craft
- Learning to take feedback without taking it personally

### Mid-weight (2–5 years)
- Owns deliverables end-to-end with light oversight
- Can extend a brand system into new contexts
- Brings options, not single answers; defends choices
- Starts mentoring juniors informally

### Senior (5–8 years)
- Leads projects, sets visual direction within strategy
- Builds brand systems from scratch
- Holds the bar across the team's output
- Trusted to present to clients without a CD in the room

### Lead / Head of Design (8+ years)
- Sets the visual standard for the studio or department
- Hires, mentors, and shapes design culture
- Represents design in commercial and strategic conversations
- Often less hands-on; the multiplier matters more than the output

## Art Director

**Core skills:** concept, image-making, casting, set/location judgment, working with photographers and illustrators, treatment writing
**Common tools:** Figma, Adobe CC, Frame.io, Milanote, mood-boarding tools
**Typical reporting line:** Creative Director

### Mid-weight (3–5 years)
- Brings a clear visual idea to a brief; can write a treatment
- Directs a shoot day with a photographer alongside a producer
- Working knowledge of motion, type, photography, and craft

### Senior (5–8 years)
- Owns the visual concept across a campaign
- Comfortable directing high-stakes shoots and commanding a room
- Pairs naturally with a senior copywriter

### Lead / ECD-track (8+ years)
- Defines the visual identity of multiple campaigns simultaneously
- Sets the bar for craft across the team
- Picks fights with clients on behalf of the work

## Copywriter

**Core skills:** concept, headline craft, long-copy, voice, structural editing, taste in tone
**Common tools:** Google Docs, Notion, Figma (for in-context copy), Grammarly (use sparingly)
**Typical reporting line:** Creative Director or Head of Strategy

### Junior (0–2 years)
- Writes within a clear brief and a defined voice
- Knows the difference between body copy and a headline
- Working on speed and on receiving feedback

### Mid-weight (2–5 years)
- Owns concept work alongside an art director
- Adapts voice across channels without losing it
- Pitches lines they're willing to defend

### Senior (5–8 years)
- Sets the tone of voice for clients and campaigns
- Strategic enough to argue against the brief when the brief is wrong
- Mentors juniors on craft, not just typos

### Lead (8+ years)
- Owns voice across multiple clients
- Pairs with senior ADs to lead on concept
- Often writes the keynote line that sells the idea

## Strategist (brand / creative / digital)

**Core skills:** synthesis, audience insight, framework thinking, pitch craft, comfort with ambiguity
**Common tools:** Notion, Figma (for strategy on a page), Miro, Google Slides
**Typical reporting line:** Head of Strategy or Managing Director

### Junior / Strategist (1–3 years)
- Conducts research and synthesises clearly
- Writes briefs that creatives can actually use
- Learning to defend a point of view in a room

### Senior Strategist (4–7 years)
- Owns strategy on multiple accounts
- Co-leads pitches with creative leads
- Translates client business problems into creative briefs

### Strategy Director / Head of Strategy (7+ years)
- Sets the strategic POV of the studio
- Trusted to advise client leadership
- Shapes proposition and proposition development across the agency

## Account Manager / Account Director

**Core skills:** client management, project oversight, commercial sense, conflict de-escalation, scoping
**Common tools:** Notion, Asana / Linear / Monday, Google Workspace, Slack
**Typical reporting line:** Account Director → Managing Director

### Account Executive (0–2 years)
- Supports an AM, takes minutes, owns small pieces of an account
- Learning to read client mood and project signals

### Account Manager (2–5 years)
- Owns the day-to-day on small/mid clients
- Writes status updates, manages scope conversations
- Comfortable saying no to scope creep with the AD's backing

### Senior Account Manager / Account Director (5–8 years)
- Owns the relationship on significant accounts
- Drives commercials — scoping, change orders, retainer renewals
- Trusted by clients as a peer, not a gatekeeper

### Group Account Director / Business Director (8+ years)
- Owns a portfolio of accounts and the commercials behind them
- Shapes how the studio serves clients structurally

## Producer

**Core skills:** scheduling, budget management, supplier management, calm under pressure, problem-solving
**Common tools:** Notion, Asana / Linear, Excel/Sheets, Frame.io for review cycles
**Typical reporting line:** Head of Production or Executive Producer

### Junior Producer (0–2 years)
- Owns small projects end-to-end
- Coordinating freelancers and suppliers
- Learning when to escalate and when to absorb

### Producer (2–5 years)
- Owns mid-size productions including shoots
- Manages budget against scope, flags slippage early

### Senior Producer / EP (5+ years)
- Owns large or complex productions
- Sets up production processes for the studio
- Trusted to bring a project home through chaos

## Developer (front-end / full-stack — agency context)

**Core skills:** HTML/CSS craft, JavaScript fluency, framework knowledge (React/Vue/Svelte), CMS integration, performance, accessibility
**Common tools:** VS Code, Git, Figma (handoff), Node, Vercel/Netlify, common headless CMSes
**Typical reporting line:** Tech Lead or Head of Engineering

### Junior (0–2 years)
- Builds components within an existing system
- Strong on craft and accessibility, learning architecture

### Mid (2–5 years)
- Owns features end-to-end; comfortable across the stack at agency level
- Translates designs into pixel-tight, accessible code

### Senior (5–8 years)
- Architects the front-end of a project
- Sets standards for performance, accessibility, code quality

### Lead / Head of Engineering (8+ years)
- Sets technical direction, makes platform decisions
- Hires, mentors, raises the bar across the team

---

## Motion Designer

**Core skills:** animation principles, timing, sound design sense, type in motion, comp craft
**Common tools:** After Effects, Cinema 4D / Blender, Figma, Frame.io
**Typical reporting line:** Design Lead or Head of Motion

### Junior (0–2 years)
- Animates within an existing system
- Strong on timing and craft; learning concept

### Mid (2–5 years)
- Owns motion deliverables end-to-end
- Can take a static identity and bring it to life

### Senior (5+ years)
- Defines motion language for brands
- Pairs with art direction on campaigns

## Other / non-standard roles

For roles like Creative Technologist, Brand Designer, Content Designer, Service
Designer, UX Researcher (creative agency context):

- Map the closest 1–2 templates above and combine relevant elements
- Note explicitly what's unique about the role in the JD
- Watch for unicorn risk — non-standard often means "we want one person doing the
  work of two"
- Be honest about what you're prioritising: design taste, technical depth, research
  rigour, or production craft. You can't have all four at the same level.
