# Tension, Insight, and the Single-Minded Thought

This file is the operational heart of the skill. It covers the four
pressure-points that separate a real creative brief from a spec document:

1. **The Tension** — the gap that drives the work
2. **The Real Objective** (vs the stated objective)
3. **Audience Depth** — beyond demographics
4. **The Single-Minded Thought** — the one sentence the team rallies around

Every brief produced by this skill should pass through all four of these. A
brief missing any one of them is a brief the creative team can't work from.

---

## 1. The Tension

### What it is

The tension is the gap between where the brand is and where it needs to be.
Or between what the audience currently thinks/feels/does and what they
need to think/feel/do. Or between the internal story and the external
reality.

Without a tension, there's no reason for the work to exist. Good briefs
lead with tension; bad briefs bury it under deliverables.

### Where to find it

Four sources, in order:

**Source 1: The brief inputs themselves**
Read the raw input for phrases like:
- "...but we..." (the 'but' signals tension)
- "...even though..."
- "We're seen as X but we need to be seen as Y"
- "The business has changed but the brand hasn't"
- "Our audience assumes X when actually..."
- "We know what we are internally but externally..."

**Source 2: Upstream skill synthesis**
- `trend-report` — trends that are eroding the brand's current position
- `competitor-audit` — spaces competitors have taken, spaces left open
- `source-scrape` evidence mode — hard data that contradicts the client's self-image
- `pitch-research` prospect dossier — the reason the agency was approached in the first place

**Source 3: The brain**
- `clients.md` — what this client's ongoing challenges are
- `learnings.md` — past projects with similar tensions
- `prospects/{slug}.md` — if this came from a pitch, the hook was the tension

**Source 4: The inferred gap**
If no tension is stated or derivable, synthesise one by asking: *"Why is
this project happening now? What would be true if it succeeded that isn't
true today?"* The answer to "why now" is almost always the tension.

### How to write it

The tension should be a **single sentence**, 15-30 words, with a clear gap:

> **Bad:** "Nike Air needs a refresh."
>
> **Bad:** "The brand needs to feel more modern to younger audiences."
>
> **Good:** "Nike Air was built for performance but lives in a culture that cares more about meaning than specs — performance authority is no longer enough."
>
> **Good:** "Our audience already trusts us for practical software — but they've started reaching for competitors for work that matters, and we don't know why."
>
> **Good:** "We're the category leader by volume and the challenger by perception — customers buy us but don't love us."

The tension should make the creative team lean in. If it makes them shrug,
rewrite.

### The tension pressure test

After drafting, ask:
1. **Is it a real gap, or just a description?** Descriptions are static; gaps imply movement.
2. **Does it contain both sides?** A tension without two sides is a complaint.
3. **Is it specific to this brand, or could it apply to any brand in the category?** If it's generic, it's not the tension.
4. **Does it explain why this project, why now?** If not, keep digging.

If the tension fails any pressure test, flag it in the output and offer the
user the chance to sharpen it.

### When there's no tension

Sometimes the project really is a maintenance or execution job with no real
tension — a standard phase 2 rollout, a retainer content request, a refresh
with no strategic shift. In those cases, write this explicitly:

> "**Tension:** This is an execution phase, not a strategic one — the
> tension was resolved in the previous project. The brief focuses on craft
> and consistency, not breakthrough thinking."

This is honest reporting. Don't fabricate a tension where one doesn't exist.

## 2. Real Objective vs Stated Objective

### The problem

Clients often state **deliverables** as **objectives**:

| Stated "objective" | What it actually is |
|---|---|
| "Redesign the website" | A deliverable |
| "Launch a new campaign" | A deliverable |
| "Refresh the brand" | A deliverable |
| "Build a design system" | A deliverable |

A deliverable is what gets made. An objective is what the deliverable is
**for**. The creative team can't do meaningful work from a deliverable
statement alone.

### How to separate them

For every stated objective, ask: *"And why?"* Keep asking until you get to
something that isn't a deliverable.

Example:

> **Stated:** "We want to redesign the website."
>
> **Why?** "The current one is dated."
>
> **Why does that matter?** "Our customers are choosing competitors."
>
> **Why are they doing that?** "They think we've stopped investing in the product."
>
> **Real objective:** "Make the website signal active product investment so customers stop defecting to competitors on perception grounds."

The real objective is almost always the third or fourth "why" deep.

### How to write the objective section

Write both:

> **Stated objective (from client):** Redesign the website.
>
> **Real objective (what it's for):** Signal active product investment so
> customers stop defecting to competitors on perception grounds.
>
> **Success criterion:** Customer retention on the "we feel like they're
> investing in the product" attribute rises in the next quarterly NPS pulse.

The creative team works against the real objective. The stated objective
becomes the deliverable in the scope section.

### Measurability

Every real objective should have at least one measurable success criterion.
If it doesn't, ask the client: *"How will we know this worked in 6 months?"*

If they can't answer, the objective isn't complete enough yet. Flag as an
Open Question before any creative work begins.

### When the objective is genuinely fuzzy

Some creative work has legitimately qualitative objectives ("feel more
premium", "be more distinctive"). In those cases:

1. Accept the qualitative framing
2. Try to attach a proxy metric ("premium" might proxy through "willingness
   to pay 15% more" or "unaided brand ranking in premium set")
3. If no proxy exists, note that measurement is qualitative and agree how
   the team will evaluate subjectively ("CD review against premium
   references we all agree on")

Don't force quantification where it doesn't fit. Don't skip the conversation.

## 3. Audience Depth

### The "millennials" problem

"Millennials" is not an audience. Neither is "young urban professionals",
"Gen Z", "our customers", "women 35-54", or "decision-makers in SMEs". These
are demographic slices, not audiences.

An audience is **a specific group of people with a specific relationship to
the brand, doing a specific thing, with a specific motivation**.

### The 4-layer audience check

For every audience statement, check 4 layers:

**Layer 1: Who are they specifically?**
Not demographics. Behaviours, situations, choices they make.

> **Bad:** "Millennials"
> **Better:** "People who buy running shoes more than once a year"
> **Best:** "People who've run 3+ marathons and are now training for their first ultra"

**Layer 2: What's their relationship to the brand/category?**
Current users? Lapsed users? Prospective users? Category rejectors? Indifferent?

> "They've used the product in the past but drifted to a competitor in the last 12 months"

**Layer 3: What are they doing when they encounter the work?**
Context matters more than demographics. The same person behaves differently
in different contexts.

> "Mid-scroll on Instagram, not looking for anything specific, waiting for a train"

**Layer 4: What do they want (that this work can give them)?**
Desires, not features. Emotional, not functional.

> "To feel like the tools they use for work reflect the taste they have outside of work"

### The audience test

A specific-enough audience description should answer all 4 layers. If it
only answers Layer 1 (demographics), it's a persona placeholder, not an
audience.

### Writing the audience section

```markdown
## Target Audience

**Primary:**

{Layer 1 — behavioural description}

**What they currently think/do about the brand/category:**
{Layer 2 — relationship}

**Where and when this work will reach them:**
{Layer 3 — context}

**What they actually want (that we can address):**
{Layer 4 — desire}

**What we know about them that's non-obvious:**
{The insight — see section 4 below}
```

### The insight

The insight is a non-obvious truth about the audience that unlocks the
creative. It's not the same as audience data. Data says "75% of our users
open the app in the morning." An insight says "they're checking to feel
prepared, not to learn something new — the morning check-in is a ritual,
not a task."

Insights come from:
- Listening to real customers (quotes, reviews, support tickets)
- Ethnography and observation
- `source-scrape` in evidence mode for behavioural data
- `/creativestack:trend-report` for cultural shifts
- Counter-intuitive findings in research

**If there's no insight in the brief inputs**, the skill should flag it:

> "This brief has no audience insight — just demographics. The creative
> team needs at least one non-obvious truth about this audience before
> they start. Suggested next step: a quick ethnographic conversation or a
> `/creativestack:source-scrape` evidence-mode query on this audience's
> behaviour."

### Multiple audiences

If the brief has primary and secondary audiences, run the 4-layer check on
each. Don't let the primary pass and the secondary be a vague afterthought.

## 4. The Single-Minded Thought (The Proposition)

### What it is

One sentence. The thing the team rallies around when the work gets hard.
The thing the brief comes back to when a decision is difficult.

Sometimes called "the proposition", sometimes "the big idea", sometimes
"the thought". The terminology matters less than the discipline: **one
sentence, sharp enough to stop other sentences from sneaking in**.

### What it is NOT

- **Not a tagline.** Taglines are consumer-facing copy. Single-minded
  thoughts are internal.
- **Not a brand positioning.** Positioning is the long-term strategic
  space; the single-minded thought is project-specific.
- **Not a list.** If it has bullets, it's not single-minded.
- **Not a deliverable.** "We'll deliver a campaign" is not a thought.
- **Not a feature.** "We have the fastest engine" is a feature, not a thought.

### How to write it

Three templates that work:

**Template A: "To [audience], [brand] is the [category] that [differentiation]"**

> "To runners training for their first ultra, Nike Air is the shoe brand that honours their ambition without asking them to prove it yet."

**Template B: "When [audience] [context/behaviour], we want them to [desired response]"**

> "When overwhelmed professionals open our app in the morning, we want them to feel prepared for the day, not confronted by a to-do list."

**Template C: The provocation**

Some teams prefer a contrarian sentence designed to shock the team into
new thinking:

> "What if this wasn't a bank at all?"
>
> "Stop designing for shoppers. Design for enthusiasts."
>
> "The real product is the community, not the software."

Provocations work when the team is stuck in category-typical thinking. Use
sparingly — they can come across as clever rather than clear.

### Pressure tests for the single-minded thought

After drafting, run these 4 tests:

1. **Could two people read this and reach the same conclusion about what to make?** If not, it's too vague.
2. **Could this sentence be said by any competitor?** If yes, it's not specific enough.
3. **Does it force a creative choice?** A good thought makes some directions
   obvious and others impossible. A bad thought leaves all directions open.
4. **Does it survive translation?** Read it aloud. Does it sound like
   something a human would say, or like corporate language?

If the thought fails any test, rewrite.

### When the thought depends on client input

Sometimes the skill can't generate the thought from the inputs alone — it
needs client conversation. In those cases, write a **candidate thought**
and frame it as a question:

> **Candidate single-minded thought:** "Nike Air honours ambition without
> asking you to prove it."
>
> **This needs client alignment before the creative team can commit.**
> Suggested next step: share the brief's tension and this candidate
> thought with the client — get them to say "yes, that's it" or "no, it's
> more like X" before the team starts work.

The skill should not fabricate a thought that hasn't been validated. But
it should push to articulate one, even as a candidate.

### The creative question (optional)

Some briefs benefit from an additional "creative question" — a provocation
for the team to answer. This isn't required and should only be used if it
sharpens thinking rather than muddying it.

Examples:

> "How can this website feel more like a morning coffee than a morning meeting?"
>
> "If this brand were a person, what would they refuse to say?"
>
> "What's the one thing we'd show a competitor that would make them jealous?"

If used, put it at the end of the brief, after the thought, as a closing
provocation for the team to hold onto.

## Putting it together

A brief that passes through all four pressure points ends up with:

- **A clear tension** that explains why the project exists
- **A real objective** separated from the stated deliverable
- **An audience** described in behaviour, context, and desire (not just demographics)
- **An insight** that gives the team something non-obvious to work with
- **A single-minded thought** the whole team can rally around

This is what distinguishes a creative brief from a spec document. The
skill's job is to enforce the distinction — politely but firmly — and to
flag when any of the four is weak.

The completeness rubric (`completeness-rubric.md`) scores each of these
explicitly. A brief that aces deliverables but fails the tension will
still score low overall — because without tension the team has nothing
to make work from.
