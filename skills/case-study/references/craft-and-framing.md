# Craft and Framing

This file covers:
1. Narrative patterns — the story shapes a case study can take
2. The "What was hard" section — the honesty discipline
3. Metrics framing — six comparison frameworks for presenting results
4. Unexpected outcomes — often the most compelling part
5. The asset brief — what visuals to commission

The goal is to help the skill produce case studies that creatives would
actually publish, not generic "challenge/approach/solution/results"
write-ups that read the same across every agency.

---

## 1. Narrative patterns

A case study has a story shape. The current skill uses the default
"challenge/approach/solution/results" scaffold for every project, which is
fine but flat. The best case studies pick a **narrative pattern** that
serves the specific project.

Pick the pattern that fits the work. Don't force every project into the
same shape.

### Pattern A: Before / After (the transformation)

**Use when:** The work produced a visible transformation the reader can
see or measure. Most common pattern, reliable.

**Shape:**
1. **Before** — what the client's state was (described specifically, with
   imagery if possible)
2. **The break** — the moment of decision or insight
3. **The work** — what was made
4. **After** — what the client's state became (with evidence)

**Example framing:**
> "Before the refresh, the brand was on six different colour palettes
> across six business units. Customers couldn't tell it was one company.
> After: one system, held across every touchpoint, audited and governed."

### Pattern B: Tension / Resolution

**Use when:** The project was driven by a specific tension that the work
resolved. Works well for positioning and strategic work.

**Shape:**
1. **The tension** — a clear gap or contradiction the brand was living with
2. **Why it mattered** — business stakes
3. **The resolution** — how the work resolved the tension
4. **Proof** — evidence the resolution worked

**Example framing:**
> "The brand wanted to be seen as premium but couldn't afford premium
> photography. The old solution was to shoot cheap and retouch heavily,
> which looked uncanny. The new solution was a type-led system that made
> photography optional."

### Pattern C: Code-break (the distinctive move)

**Use when:** The work deliberately broke a category convention. Requires
an existing competitor audit or clear category context.

**Shape:**
1. **The category code** — what most brands in this space did
2. **The refusal** — why the client didn't want to follow the code
3. **The break** — what the work did instead
4. **The result** — how the break landed

**Example framing:**
> "Every running shoe brand shoots runners mid-motion, low angle, with
> blur. We shot portraits — runners in stillness, before and after the
> run. The break made the brand feel like a life, not a spectacle."

### Pattern D: Process reveal

**Use when:** The craft of the work is itself the story. Best for
identity, design system, and product work. Strong for awards submissions.

**Shape:**
1. **The problem** — brief version, not the focus
2. **The process** — step by step, showing how decisions were made
3. **The specifics** — type choices, colour decisions, system rules,
   technical craft
4. **The outcome** — what the process produced

**Process reveal case studies are longer** and work best in editorial
contexts (design press, awards, long-form portfolio). Not suitable for a
short credentials deck.

### Pattern E: Unexpected outcome

**Use when:** The work solved something different from what it was
commissioned to solve. Often the most compelling story, underused.

**Shape:**
1. **The brief** — what the client asked for
2. **The twist** — what the team discovered along the way
3. **The real work** — what ended up being made (different from the brief)
4. **The result** — why the twist mattered

**Example framing:**
> "We were briefed to redesign the website. What we found in user research
> was that the website was fine — the problem was the onboarding email.
> We convinced the client to let us rewrite the emails first. Conversion
> doubled in 6 weeks. Then we redesigned the website."

Unexpected outcome case studies make the team look thoughtful and the
relationship look strong. They're also honest — most good work has a
twist. Surface it.

### Pattern F: The long relationship

**Use when:** The case study is about a multi-year client relationship,
not a single project. Strong positioning for agencies pitching retainer
work.

**Shape:**
1. **Year 1** — how the relationship started, what the first project was
2. **Evolution** — how the work deepened over time
3. **Compound** — what the relationship has produced collectively
4. **Now** — where the work currently is

Long relationship case studies build credibility differently. They prove
sustained value, which is rarer and more defensible than a single
breakthrough.

### Which pattern to pick

The skill should read the extracted material and suggest a pattern based
on what's strongest in the evidence:

| Signal in the evidence | Suggested pattern |
|---|---|
| Clear before/after transformation with visual evidence | Before/After |
| Strong tension in the brief + clear resolution in results | Tension/Resolution |
| Competitor audit shows code-break opportunity taken | Code-break |
| Post-mortem or team notes celebrate craft detail | Process reveal |
| Meeting log shows the work shifted direction mid-project | Unexpected outcome |
| Project has multiple phases across 2+ years | Long relationship |

Surface the suggestion and let the user confirm:

> "Based on the evidence, this project works best as a Before/After
> narrative — the brand's identity system was fragmented before, unified
> after, with clear visual proof. Continue with this pattern, or try
> another?"

## 2. The "What was hard" section

**This is the single biggest thing missing from generic case studies.**
Great case studies are honest about the struggle. They show the team
solved problems, not just executed a brief.

Most case studies hide the difficulty because the client wants to look
decisive and the agency wants to look competent. The result is flat
marketing that doesn't persuade anyone.

### The principle

Show the struggle, then show how the team worked through it. Never show a
project that was "smooth from start to finish" — no real project is, and
readers know it.

### What to include

Look for these signals in the extracted material:

- **Open → Resolved decisions** in project state: each unresolved-then-resolved decision was a hard call the team made
- **Risks that were flagged and addressed**: risks that appeared and went away tell a story
- **Engagement health trajectory with a dip**: `enthusiastic → cautious → enthusiastic` means something happened in the middle
- **Post-mortem "what didn't work" section**: direct material
- **Meeting log with any tension or debate**: the hardest moments usually show up in meetings
- **Copy deck that went through 3+ versions**: version churn means the team rewrote things until they worked

### How to frame it

**Don't:**
- Blame the client
- Complain about scope
- Vent about timelines
- Attack individuals

**Do:**
- Name a specific challenge
- Show the team's response
- Show the resolution
- Credit what made the resolution possible (team, client collaboration,
  technical craft)

### Example framings

**Bad framing:**
> "The client was difficult and changed their mind multiple times during
> the project, forcing us to restart twice."

**Good framing:**
> "Halfway through concept development, the category shifted. A major
> competitor launched a platform that used a visual direction close to
> ours. We had a choice: hold our course and risk looking derivative, or
> restart with a sharper distinction. The team voted unanimously for the
> restart. Six weeks later, the work launched and immediately stood apart."

**Bad framing:**
> "The timeline was unrealistic and caused a lot of stress for the team."

**Good framing:**
> "We had 8 weeks for work that typically takes 14. To make it work, we
> shifted to a tighter team of 3 seniors instead of a broader team of 6,
> and ran design and production in parallel instead of sequentially. The
> tradeoff: fewer concept rounds, more confidence required from the CD
> on the first direction. It held up."

### When to skip this section

Some projects really didn't have a hard moment. If the extracted material
shows no resolved decisions, no sentiment dips, no version churn, and no
post-mortem struggles, skip the "What was hard" section rather than
fabricating difficulty. Honesty matters more than the pattern.

## 3. Metrics framing — six comparison frameworks

The current skill's metrics framing is good but shallow. Here are six
comparison frameworks for presenting metrics. Pick the framework that
makes the metric most impressive and honest for this project.

### Framework 1: Percentage change

> "40% lift in unaided awareness"

**Best when:** The base number is small or unimpressive. "From 25 to 35"
sounds less impressive than "40% increase."

**Watch for:** Percentage changes from very small bases can be misleading
("1000% increase" from 1 to 10). Always include the base if the number
is small.

### Framework 2: Absolute number

> "1.2 million people watched the launch video"

**Best when:** The absolute number is large enough to be impressive on
its own. A big number doesn't need a percentage.

**Watch for:** Absolute numbers without context can feel like vanity
metrics. Always include the *so what* ("1.2M views — the most-watched
launch in the brand's history" or "1.2M views, 40% above the previous
record").

### Framework 3: Comparison to the client's own baseline

> "Doubled the previous year's conversion rate"

**Best when:** The client has a known historical baseline. Self-comparison
is always the strongest comparison because it accounts for context the
industry benchmark can't.

**Watch for:** Only use if the baseline is verifiable. "Doubled" with no
source feels weak.

### Framework 4: Comparison to industry benchmark

> "3x the category average click-through rate"

**Best when:** The category has a known benchmark that the reader
recognises. Useful for unfamiliar categories where absolute numbers
mean little.

**Watch for:** The benchmark needs to be real and cite-able. Made-up
benchmarks ("according to industry standards") are weak.

### Framework 5: Comparison to the stated goal

> "Beat the year-end target by 6 months"

**Best when:** The brief had a stated goal and the work exceeded it. This
framework is powerful because it closes the narrative loop — the case
study opened with a goal and the work beat it.

**Watch for:** Only works if the goal was actually stated in the brief.
Pulling this from `projects/{slug}-brief.md § Success criteria`.

### Framework 6: Time-to-result

> "Achieved in 6 weeks, against a 6-month expected timeline"

**Best when:** Speed is the story. Works for products that took off
quickly, campaigns that overdelivered fast, or launches where the team
beat a deadline.

**Watch for:** Time-to-result is weaker when the result itself is modest.
"Achieved X in record time" sells X, not speed.

### Choosing a framework per metric

For each metric in the case study, the skill should pick the strongest
framework and explain why in a small side note the writer can keep or cut:

```markdown
**Metric:** 1.2M views

**Frameworks considered:**
- Absolute: "1.2M views" — strong on its own
- Time-based: "1.2M views in the first week" — strong, makes the speed the story
- Baseline: "1.2M views — 4x the previous launch video" — strongest, self-comparison

**Chosen framing:** "1.2M views in the first week — 4x the brand's previous launch video record"

**Why:** Combines absolute, time-based, and baseline. Tells the reader
the number is big, fast, and better than the client's own history.
```

Combining frameworks (within reason) produces the strongest framings.

### Qualitative results

Not every project has hard metrics. When hard numbers aren't available,
frame qualitative results with specificity:

- **Client retention** — "The client extended the partnership into a
  3-year retainer after the launch."
- **Internal adoption** — "The design system was adopted across all 14
  business units within the first quarter, with zero central enforcement."
- **Industry recognition** — "The work was featured in [publication] and
  included in [award longlist]."
- **Follow-on work** — "The client commissioned a phase 2 within 30 days
  of launch."
- **Team impact** — "The project was the agency's most-referenced case
  study in new business meetings for the following 12 months."

Qualitative results are legitimate. Frame them specifically and they
carry weight.

### When metrics are weak

Some projects don't produce impressive metrics, and fabricating ones is
a firing offence. In that case:

- **Be honest** — don't invent numbers
- **Lead with qualitative** — retention, recognition, follow-on work
- **Frame craft** — "The work was featured in the case study collection
  of [publication]" is legitimate
- **Flag internally** — the skill can note "This project has thin metrics.
  Consider whether to publish it or strengthen the results before writing."

Don't publish a case study with weak metrics and hope nobody notices.
Either strengthen the results or wait.

## 4. Unexpected outcomes

**First-class section, often the most compelling part of any case study.**

Most projects produce an unexpected outcome — something the team didn't
expect, didn't target, but that turned out to matter. These outcomes make
case studies memorable.

### Where to find unexpected outcomes

- **Post-mortem notes** — "Actually, the most interesting result was..."
- **Client follow-up emails** — clients often share outcomes the team
  didn't measure
- **Internal team observations** — "One thing I noticed after launch was..."
- **Long-term results** — outcomes that emerged 3-6 months after the
  project closed (not captured in the immediate results section)

### How to frame them

Unexpected outcomes get their own short section:

```markdown
## What we didn't expect

{The unexpected thing, specifically. Not vague celebration — specific
surprise.}

{Why it matters — what it says about the work or the audience.}
```

**Example:**

> "What we didn't expect: the campaign's biggest audience turned out to
> be the parents of the target demographic. Their share rate on social
> was 3x the primary target's. The brand is now planning a follow-up
> series that leans into the parent audience explicitly. What started
> as a Gen Z campaign became a family one."

### Unexpected outcomes as the hook

If the unexpected outcome is strong enough, it can become the narrative
hook for the whole case study (Pattern E: Unexpected outcome). The skill
should recognise when this is the case and restructure accordingly.

## 5. The asset brief

Every case study needs visuals. Generic case studies forget this until
publication time and end up with mediocre imagery. The skill should
produce an **asset brief** alongside the case study — what visuals to
commission or produce.

### Asset brief structure

```markdown
## Asset Brief for this Case Study

### Hero image (must-have)
**What:** {specific visual the hero image should show}
**Style:** {photography direction, from the brand tone & personality if available}
**Source:** {existing photography from the project OR commission new}
**Dimensions:** {based on publication target — web: 2400×1600, print: high-res TIFF, etc.}

### Supporting images (3-6)
1. **{What to show}** — {why it matters for the narrative}
2. **{What to show}** — {why}
3. **{What to show}** — {why}

### Optional elements
- **Video:** {if applicable — hero film, process film, client testimonial}
- **Diagrams:** {if process reveal pattern — system diagrams, decision frameworks}
- **Before/After:** {if before/after pattern — paired images}

### Source inventory
- Images we have: {from project files, brand assets, etc.}
- Images we need: {gap list to commission or produce}
```

### Asset brief pulls from the project

The skill can pre-populate the asset brief from:

- **Copy deck** — the key deliverables that shipped (web screens, campaign
  frames, brand applications)
- **Competitor audit visual captures** — useful for "category codes"
  showing what was broken
- **Brief's deliverables list** — the commissioned work
- **Case studies in the brain** — existing imagery from similar projects
  that could be reused

### Asset brief as a separate deliverable

The asset brief isn't published in the case study — it's a **production
deliverable** for the team. Output it as a separate section the user can
hand to the photographer, designer, or production manager.

## 6. Combining patterns in the output

A single case study doesn't use all six narrative patterns, all six
metrics frameworks, and every section. The skill should pick what's
strongest and leave the rest. The principle:

**Produce the case study the evidence supports. Don't force completeness.**

If the extracted material is strong on narrative and weak on metrics,
write a narrative-led case study. If it's strong on metrics and weak on
process, write a metrics-led case study. Let the evidence shape the form.

The multi-length output (from `brain-case-study-schema.md`) means the
same source material can produce different shapes for different audiences.
The long-form version includes more; the 1-paragraph version picks the
single strongest framing.
