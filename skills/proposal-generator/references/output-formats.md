# Proposal Output Formats

Templates for the three output variants. Generated by `/proposal-generator`.
Always generate the full proposal + one-page summary together. Use the RFP variant
when adapting to a formal RFP template.

---

## Full Proposal

```markdown
# Proposal: {Project/Client Name}
**Date:** {today}
**Prepared by:** {agency name from profile.md}
**Version:** 1.0

## Executive Summary
{2-3 paragraphs. The first sentence must be specific to *this* brief, not a generic
positioning statement. State what the client needs, the high-level approach, and the
single sharpest reason you're the right fit. Cut anything that could appear in any
other agency's exec summary.}

## Understanding
{Restate the brief in your own words — proves you've listened. Pull objectives,
audience, and deliverables from the project state Brief if available. Where the brief
is ambiguous, state your interpretation explicitly so misalignment surfaces early.}

## Approach

### Phase 1: {Phase Name} — {duration}
{What happens in this phase, key activities, outputs, who's involved.}

### Phase 2: {Phase Name} — {duration}
...

{Use methodology.md for default phase structure if it exists. Otherwise build from
the project type. Always end with a final delivery / handover phase.}

## Deliverables
| Deliverable | Format | Included |
|-------------|--------|----------|
| {deliverable} | {format} | Yes / Optional |

State revision rounds explicitly per phase. Vague revision policy is the #1 cause of
scope creep.

## Timeline
| Phase | Duration | Key Milestone |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| {phase} | {weeks} | {milestone} |

**Total duration:** {weeks}

{If timeline-generator was run, reference it: "See the detailed timeline with
dependencies and slip scenarios generated separately."}

## Team
| Role | Person/Level | Involvement | Relevant Experience |
|------|-------------|-------------|---------------------|
| {role} | {name or level} | {hours / % / phase} | {1-line from team.md} |

{Don't just list names — show why these specific people are right for this brief.
If team.md has bios, pull a relevant sentence.}

## Risks & Assumptions

The most important section in the whole proposal. Make every load-bearing assumption
explicit. The clearer this section, the fewer fights later.

**We are assuming:**
- {assumption 1 — e.g., "The client provides existing brand assets, photography, and copy in source format by week 1."}
- {assumption 2 — e.g., "A single approver from the client side is named at kickoff."}
- {assumption 3 — e.g., "Stakeholder feedback consolidated to one voice per round, not aggregated raw."}

**Risks we're flagging:**
- {risk — e.g., "Three rounds of revision implied but not stated. Default to two rounds per phase unless agreed otherwise."}
- {risk from gut check — e.g., "Decision-maker not named. Recommend confirming before kickoff."}

**What's not included:**
- {explicit out-of-scope item 1}
- {explicit out-of-scope item 2}

## Investment

{Pricing structure based on the selected model — see Pricing Model Templates below.}

**Payment terms:**
- {Deposit %} on signature
- {Milestone schedule}
- Final payment on delivery / handover
- Late payment: {statutory interest or agreed terms}

## Intellectual Property and Usage Rights

{State explicitly:}
- Who owns the work product on final payment
- Scope of usage (geographic, time, channel)
- Whether source files are included or final exports only
- Photography / illustration / music / talent licensing terms (if applicable)

## Why Us

{2-3 sentences. Specific and provable, not aspirational. Reference the strongest 1-2
case studies, not all of them. The differentiation must be something a competitor
can't honestly claim.}

**Relevant Work:**
{2-3 case studies max. For each:}

**{Case Study Name}** — {client industry / type}
{1-2 sentences. Lead with the outcome, not the process.}

## Competitive Differentiation
{Why you over others. Pull from profile.md positioning, case-studies.md proof points,
team.md specific expertise, and pitch-research output if available. Be specific.}

## Next Steps
1. {Specific next step — e.g., "Confirm decision-maker and approval process"}
2. {Next step — e.g., "30-min alignment call to walk through this proposal"}
3. {Next step}
```

## One-Page Summary

```markdown
# {Project/Client Name} — Proposal Summary

**{Agency name}** | {Date}

**The brief:** {2-3 sentences restating what the client needs. Specific.}

**Our approach:** {3-4 sentences on how you'll tackle it. No generic agency-speak.}

**Key phases:**
1. {Phase} — {duration}
2. {Phase} — {duration}
3. {Phase} — {duration}

**Team:** {key roles and names, one line}

**Timeline:** {total duration} | **Investment:** {range — never single number}

**Why us:** {2-3 sentences — the sharpest version of your edge, with one specific
case study reference if available}

**Key assumption:** {the single biggest assumption from the full proposal — surface
it here so it doesn't get buried}

**Next step:** {single clear action}
```

## RFP-Adapted Variant

When responding to a formal RFP with prescribed format:

1. **Read the RFP's table of contents and section headings.** Use exactly those, in
   exactly that order. Don't impose your default structure.
2. **Match their scoring criteria.** If the RFP has weighted scoring (Approach 30%,
   Team 25%, Pricing 25%, Differentiation 20%), allocate proposal real estate
   proportionally. The Approach section gets the most space.
3. **Match their format requirements.** Page count, font, line spacing, file format,
   section length limits — these are often pass/fail filters before content is read.
4. **Answer their questions in their order.** RFPs often embed specific questions
   in the brief. Each one needs a direct answer, not a paraphrase.
5. **Mirror their language where appropriate.** If they say "vendor", don't say
   "partner". If they say "engagement", don't say "project". Match their world.
6. **Include the standard sections last.** Risks, assumptions, IP, and what's not
   included still go in — usually as appendices or terms sheets if the RFP doesn't
   have explicit slots.

The core content (understanding, approach, team, pricing, differentiation, risks) is
the same. What changes is structure and language. Don't strip the substance.

## Pricing Model Templates

Use the model the user selected in Step 5. Always present ranges, never single numbers.

### Fixed fee
| Option | Scope | Investment |
|--------|-------|-----------|
| Option A: Core | {description} | {range} |
| Option B: Standard | {description} | {range} |

### Day rate
| Role | Day Rate | Estimated Days | Estimate |
|------|----------|----------------|----------|
| {role} | {rate} | {days range} | {total range} |

State the assumed total range explicitly. Day rate without a cap is open-ended and
clients hate it.

### Retainer
| Monthly Scope | Hours | Monthly Fee |
|---------------|-------|-------------|
| {scope description} | {hours/month} | {fee} |

State minimum term (e.g., 3-month minimum) and what happens to unused hours (roll
over for one month? expire? don't leave this ambiguous).

### Hybrid (fixed + day rate for extras)
**Fixed component:**
| Scope | Investment |
|-------|-----------|
| {core scope} | {range} |

**Variable component:**
| Role | Day Rate | Cap |
|------|----------|-----|
| {role} | {rate} | {max days before triggering re-scope} |

### Phased (fee per phase + go/no-go gates)
| Phase | Scope | Fee | Go/No-Go Decision |
|-------|-------|-----|-------------------|
| Phase 1: Discovery | {scope} | {range} | Client review before Phase 2 commits |
| Phase 2: {Name} | {scope} | {range} | Client review before Phase 3 commits |
| Phase 3: {Name} | {scope} | {range} | Final delivery |

The first phase should be paid discovery. This protects both sides when the brief
is ambiguous and lets the client opt out cheaply if the work isn't landing.
